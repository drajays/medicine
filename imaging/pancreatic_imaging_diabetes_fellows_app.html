Core takeaways for fellows

Pancreatic imaging is most diagnostic when diabetes is caused by an exocrine pancreatic disease, a developmental/monogenic pancreatic abnormality, cystic fibrosis, autoimmune pancreatitis, iron overload, pancreatic cancer, or pancreatic surgery. In type 1 and type 2 diabetes, pancreas size/fat changes are real and research-useful, but usually not standalone diagnostic tests.

1) Imaging first-line in suspected CP/type 3c

High-quality CT or MRI/MRCP is usually first-line for established chronic pancreatitis. EUS and secretin MRCP become important when cross-sectional imaging is negative but suspicion remains high.

Refs 1–17

2) Calcifications change the diabetes differential

Diffuse parenchymal or ductal calcifications/stones strongly support chronic calcific pancreatitis, TCP/FCPD, hereditary pancreatitis, and diabetes of the exocrine pancreas rather than uncomplicated T2D.

Refs 18–46

3) Small pancreas ≠ one diagnosis

A small or atrophic pancreas can occur in T1D, long-standing CP, HNF1B/MODY5, distal obstruction from pancreatic cancer, CF, post-pancreatitis diabetes, or pancreatic agenesis/hypoplasia syndromes.

Interpret clinically

4) Monogenic clues are structural + syndromic

Think HNF1B/17q12 with renal cysts, hypomagnesemia, genital tract anomalies and small/dorsal pancreas; GATA6/PDX1/PTF1A with pancreatic agenesis/hypoplasia; CEL-MODY with exocrine dysfunction, cysts or lipomatosis.

Refs 65–84

5) Fatty pancreas is a risk marker, not a label

Pancreatic steatosis/NAFPD is associated with obesity, metabolic syndrome, T2D and pancreatic cancer risk, but thresholds and causality remain uncertain. Report quantitatively when possible.

Refs 47, 59–64, 100

6) Always exclude a dangerous mimic

New-onset diabetes with weight loss, jaundice, rapid glycemic worsening, duct cut-off, distal atrophy, or a focal mass should trigger pancreatic cancer evaluation. Focal autoimmune pancreatitis can mimic cancer and may need EUS tissue.

Refs 89–96
Safety note: This is an academic educational app, not medical advice. Use local radiology protocols, renal/contrast safety rules, pregnancy/radiation precautions, and multidisciplinary review for pancreatic masses or atypical diabetes.

Imaging modalities: what each test is best at

USG / transabdominal ultrasound

  • Fast, low cost, no radiation.
  • Can show echogenic calcifications with acoustic shadow, MPD dilatation, pseudocyst, biliary obstruction.
  • Limited by bowel gas, obesity, operator skill; early CP can be missed.
USGcalcifications

CT pancreas protocol

  • Best practical test for calcifications, intraductal stones, atrophy, pseudocysts and complications.
  • Non-contrast phase helps stones/calcification; contrast phases assess masses, necrosis, vascular disease.
  • CT can be normal in early CP.
CTCCP/FCPD

MRI / MRCP

  • Best non-invasive duct map: strictures, side branches, divisum, cysts, congenital anomalies.
  • T1 signal, diffusion, delayed enhancement, T1 mapping/ECV/MRE can reflect fibrosis.
  • Secretin MRCP helps subtle ductal/exocrine dysfunction; T2*/R2* for iron, Dixon/PDFF for fat.
MRCPsecretin

EUS

  • High sensitivity for early parenchymal/ductal CP changes; can sample masses and AIP mimics.
  • Rosemont/Japanese criteria include hyperechoic foci, strands, lobularity, duct irregularity, side branches, stones.
  • Invasive; specificity and interobserver variability require clinical correlation.
EUSearly CP

Disease modules: how to teach the signatures

Chronic pancreatitis → diabetes of exocrine pancreas

  • Imaging: calcifications, ductal irregularity/stricture/dilation, atrophy, pseudocyst, fibrosis signal.
  • Endocrine clue: low/normal BMI, maldigestion, fat-soluble vitamin deficiency, recurrent pancreatitis, alcohol/tobacco/genetic risks.
  • Pitfall: T2D can coexist with CP; prove exocrine pancreatic disease and consider autoantibodies/C-peptide.

FCPD / TCP / CCP

  • Imaging: coarse ductal/parenchymal calcifications, large duct stones, MPD dilation, atrophic gland.
  • Fellow clue: lean young diabetes, abdominal pain/steatorrhea, endemic/tropical or Indian-subcontinent context.
  • Clinical issue: insulin deficiency with variable insulin sensitivity; hypoglycemia risk; pancreatic cancer vigilance.

Type 1 diabetes pancreas

  • Imaging/research: smaller pancreas, declining volume around onset, altered diffusion/shape, exocrine enzyme reductions.
  • Teaching point: supports T1D as whole-pancreas disorder but does not replace autoantibodies/C-peptide.
  • Special situation: checkpoint-inhibitor diabetes can show pancreatic atrophy.

Type 2 diabetes / fatty pancreas

  • Imaging: pancreatic fat/steatosis by MRI/MRS/Dixon/PDFF; variable size.
  • Teaching point: reflects ectopic fat biology and metabolic risk; threshold-based diagnosis is not standardized.
  • Pitfall: fat replacement from CF/CEL/lipomatosis is not the same as common metabolic steatosis.

Monogenic structural pancreas

  • HNF1B/MODY5: pancreatic hypoplasia/atrophy/dorsal agenesis + renal cysts/hypomagnesemia.
  • GATA6/PDX1/PTF1A: agenesis/hypoplasia, neonatal diabetes, congenital anomalies.
  • CEL-MODY: diabetes + exocrine dysfunction + pancreatic cysts/lipomatosis.

Structural mimics: CF, AIP, iron, cancer

  • CF: fatty atrophy and islet loss with CFRD.
  • AIP: diffuse/focal enlargement, capsule rim, delayed enhancement; cancer mimic.
  • Iron overload: low pancreatic T2*; cancer: mass/duct cut-off/distal atrophy with new-onset diabetes.

Radiology report checklist for diabetes-related pancreatic disease

Parenchyma

  • Size/volume: normal, atrophic, hypoplastic, agenesis.
  • Fatty replacement or lipomatosis.
  • Calcifications: parenchymal vs intraductal.
  • T1 signal, delayed enhancement/fibrosis, diffusion.
  • Iron/fat quantification if protocol acquired.

Ducts and anatomy

  • Main pancreatic duct diameter and irregularity.
  • Strictures, stones, side-branch ectasia.
  • Pancreas divisum or dorsal agenesis.
  • Secretin response if s-MRCP performed.
  • Bile duct dilation/double-duct sign.

Complications and mimics

  • Pseudocyst, walled-off necrosis, vascular complications.
  • Focal mass, duct cut-off, distal atrophy.
  • AIP signs: capsule rim, diffuse enlargement, delayed enhancement, other IgG4 organ disease.
  • Regional nodes, liver lesions, vascular invasion.

Six fast self-checks

1) A lean 24-year-old from South India has diabetes, steatorrhea and CT with large MPD stones.

Think FCPD/TCP/chronic calcific pancreatitis with diabetes of the exocrine pancreas. Confirm exocrine insufficiency/nutrition, assess duct anatomy, manage insulin carefully, and consider hereditary/tropical CP context.

2) Diabetes + renal cysts + hypomagnesemia + small/dorsal pancreas on MRI.

HNF1B/MODY5 or 17q12 deletion is high on the list. Consider monogenic testing and renal/genital tract/family evaluation.

3) Neonatal diabetes with pancreatic agenesis and congenital heart disease.

GATA6 is a classic consideration; PDX1/PTF1A and other neonatal diabetes genes can also produce structural pancreatic disease depending phenotype.

4) A 61-year-old has new-onset diabetes, 8-kg weight loss, and abrupt pancreatic duct cut-off.

Treat as pancreatic cancer until proven otherwise: pancreas-protocol CT/MRI and EUS tissue pathway as appropriate.

5) Low pancreatic T2* on MRI in a transfusion-dependent thalassemia patient with impaired glucose tolerance.

Pancreatic iron overload can damage beta cells. Coordinate endocrine screening with hematology chelation/iron-overload management.

6) Long-standing T1D patient has a small pancreas on research MRI.

This is consistent with T1D whole-pancreas involvement, but it is not a diagnostic test and does not usually change routine clinical classification by itself.
