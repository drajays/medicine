The endocrine workup, in six passes

Use the clinical phenotype to decide how far to investigate. Young-adult low BMD is a finding; osteoporosis is a clinical diagnosis that must be interpreted with fracture mechanism, age, sex, skeletal maturity, disease and medication exposure.

1Confirm the phenotype

Verify low-trauma fracture, site, imaging and vertebral deformity. Repeat or review DXA quality, machine and reference database.

2Read the DXA correctly

In most men <50 and premenopausal women, use Z-score. Z ≤−2.0 means below expected range for age—not an automatic diagnosis.

3History + exam

Fractures, family, puberty/menses, pregnancy/lactation, weight/eating, exercise, drugs, alcohol, smoking and chronic disease.

4Baseline labs

Bone/mineral, renal/liver, thyroid, blood count, vitamin D, PTH and urine calcium. Look for osteomalacia and renal tubular disease.

5Targeted tests

Sex hormones, celiac serology, cortisol, SPEP, phosphate-wasting studies, HIV/medication review and rare-disease testing only when indicated.

6Escalate + treat cause

Genetics, nephrology/GI/gynecology, bone biopsy or advanced imaging for selected cases. Correct the driver before bone-specific drugs when possible.

Red flags for an advanced evaluation

✓Fragility fracture before 50, especially vertebral, hip, pelvis or multiple sites
✓Z-score ≤−2.0, rapid loss or severe discordance between BMD and fracture history
✓Menstrual/sexual dysfunction, delayed puberty, infertility or low libido
✓Kidney stones, hypercalcemia, hypophosphatemia or persistent low ALP
✓Long-term glucocorticoids, anticonvulsants, TDF, GnRH agonist, AI, DMPA or heparin
✓Family history, childhood fractures, blue sclerae, dentinogenesis imperfecta or hearing loss

Do not miss the mimic

Osteomalacia is not osteoporosis. Vitamin D deficiency, phosphate wasting, renal tubular acidosis, severe malabsorption and hypophosphatasia can produce low BMD or fractures but require different treatment. A normal or high BMD also does not exclude fragile bone in hyperparathyroidism, diabetes, Cushing syndrome or HIV.

Medication caution. Before antiresorptive therapy, check calcium, phosphate, ALP, 25-OHD, kidney function and the clinical context. Persistent low ALP plus fractures/dental clues should trigger hypophosphatasia evaluation.

Cause map for a 20–40 year presentation

Start with the clusters most likely to change management. The same patient may have more than one cause.

Endocrine excess

  • Primary hyperparathyroidism / MEN1
  • Hyperthyroidism or TSH suppression
  • Cushing syndrome / exogenous glucocorticoids
  • Acromegaly or pituitary disease

Gonadal / energy axis

  • POI, FHA, anorexia nervosa
  • Male hypogonadism, Klinefelter, Kallmann
  • Pregnancy/lactation-associated osteoporosis
  • GnRH agonists, androgen deprivation, aromatase inhibitors

Nutrition / GI

  • Celiac disease and malabsorption
  • IBD, bariatric/postoperative states
  • Low BMI, low calcium/vitamin D, restrictive diet
  • Alcohol excess

Renal / mineral

  • CKD-MBD and secondary HPT
  • Hypercalciuria / nephrolithiasis
  • Renal tubular acidosis or Fanconi syndrome
  • FGF23-mediated phosphate wasting

Chronic disease / drugs

  • CF, HIV, thalassemia, diabetes
  • Rheumatic/inflammatory disease
  • Anticonvulsants, heparin, PPIs, SSRIs, TZDs
  • Systemic glucocorticoids

Primary / genetic

  • Osteogenesis imperfecta
  • LRP5/WNT1/PLS3/SGMS2 disease
  • Hypophosphatasia (low ALP)
  • Idiopathic osteoporosis after exclusion
