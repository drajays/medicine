Although he is young, this patient has several cardiovascular disease risk factors including type 1 diabetes, cigarette smoking, positive family history, and dyslipidemia (elevated LDL cholesterol and triglycerides, low HDL cholesterol). Obviously, cessation of smoking is a major modifiable risk factor (quitting before age 30 years is estimated to add approximately 10 years of life) and should be addressed, but this was not provided as an answer option. Weight control and increased physical activity should also be recommended; however, they are not sufficient by themselves to reduce cardiovascular risk, so additional therapy should be considered.

In the Diabetes Control and Complications Trial, intensified glucose control resulted in prolonged reductions of cardiovascular disease events (the so-called legacy effect). A total of 1441 participants with type 1 diabetes aged 13 to 39 years were randomly assigned to intensive glycemic control vs standard diabetes control for an average duration of 6.5 years. After completion of the intervention, all participants were offered intensive control and the clinical course of 93% of participants was followed for up to 12 more years. During the active study, the intensive group had a mean hemoglobin  $ A_{1c} $ level of 7.4% (0.074) compared with a mean hemoglobin  $ A_{1c} $ level of 9.1% (0.091) in the standard group. However, during the follow-up period, both groups achieved similar mean hemoglobin  $ A_{1c} $ levels of 8.0% (0.080) and 8.2% (0.082), respectively. Previous intensive treatment reduced the risk of cardiovascular disease events by 42% compared with risk in the control group. Thus, intensive glycemic control reduces cardiovascular disease. However, in this vignette, the patient's hemoglobin  $ A_{1c} $ level of 7.8% (0.078) is close to that attained by the intensive control group, and thus improving his glycemic control now (Answer A) is not likely to substantially reduce his cardiovascular disease risk.

Given his elevated LDL cholesterol, low HDL cholesterol, and elevated triglycerides, use of a lipid-modifying medication may be appropriate. Dyslipidemia in young adults tends to persist throughout life and is associated with increased risk of cardiovascular disease with aging. Statins have been well studied in a wide range of patients, and they reduce unfavorable cardiovascular outcomes in nearly all. Almost all guidelines recommend statin therapy for persons with LDL-cholesterol levels of 190 mg/dL or greater ( $ \geq $4.92 mmol/L), although there is some controversy as to the age at which treatment should start. However, numerous studies evaluating lifetime risk for cardiovascular disease demonstrate associations between elevated LDL cholesterol and lifetime risk. Although this patient has a relatively low 10-year risk, his lifetime risk is high. Recent guidelines suggest that individuals aged 21 years or older who have an LDL-cholesterol concentration of 190 mg/dL or greater ( $ \geq $4.92 mmol/L) should be treated with high-intensity statin therapy to reduce risk, and calculation of 10-year risk is not required. Although this patient is younger than 21 years and his LDL-cholesterol concentration is just under 190 mg/dL (4.92 mmol/L), it is reasonable to recommend statin therapy (Answer C), which, of the listed options, is the most likely to have the greatest influence on reducing his cardiovascular risk. According to a recent meta-analysis, even persons at low risk for cardiovascular disease benefit from statins, with a 38% reduction in risk of major vascular events even in patients with an estimated risk less than 5% over 5 years.

The effect of fibrates on reducing cardiovascular risk has been controversial, with some studies showing a benefit and others no longer showing a significant benefit. In general, patients with high triglycerides and low HDL-cholesterol levels are most likely to have reduced risk of cardiovascular events with fibrate therapy; however, fibrates have not been well studied in young populations and thus outcomes are unclear.

7 ANSWER: B) Pancreatitis

Acute pancreatitis (Answer B) is characterized by inflammation of the pancreas with extension of the inflammatory response to other abdominal sites. The severity of acute pancreatitis is variable—mild-to-moderate abdominal pain, pancreatic necrosis, and even catastrophic abdominal inflammation and death. The cause incidence of acute pancreatitis ranges from 4.9 to 35 per 100,000 persons, and it is a leading cause of hospitalization in the United States. Numerous factors predispose an individual to acute pancreatitis including any disease/obstruction, alcohol, hypercalcemia, and hypertriglyceridemia (as was the case in this vignette). Uncontrolled hyperglycemia in the setting of diabetes promotes the risk of hypertriglyceridemia-induced creatitis. Insulin, along with thyroid hormone, is an important regulator of lipoprotein lipase activity, a key one involved in the processing of triglyceride-rich particles including chylomicrons and VLDL. Either insurance or insulin deficiency reduces lipoprotein lipase activity and results in hypertriglyceridemia. Triglycerin�ations of 1000 mg/dL or higher (≥11.30 mmol/L) increase the risk of acute pancreatitis, although closely lower levels do not confer protection. Children with inherited disorders of lipoprotein metabolism increased risk of hypertriglyceridemia-induced pancreatitis. Patients with pancreatitis associated with glyceridemia usually present with abdominal pain, nausea, and vomiting. Typical findings on physical include eruptive xanthomas over the extensor surfaces of the extremities, buttocks, and back; lipemia and hepatosplenomegaly from fatty infiltration of the liver.

enteric ischemia (Answer E) results from decreased blood flow through the intestinal vasculature. Chronic ischemia may present with “intestinal angina” and is characterized by recurrent acute episodes of pain during periods of heightened intestinal demand, such as after oral intake. Affected patients can crampy, postprandial epigastric pain, usually within an hour of eating. Subsequently, the pain subsides.

Some affected patients develop a food aversion due to the association with pain and subsequently eight loss. Patients with foregut ischemia from celiac artery insufficiency may have nausea, vomiting.

Cholecystitis (Answer D) is an acute inflammation of the gall bladder. Symptoms usually include steady, worsening right upper quadrant pain with associated guarding, fever, and nausea. Affected patients usually have leukocytosis and a positive Murphy sign, which is associated with increased pain on palpation of the gall bladder, and patients catching their breath during inspiration. Such patients are usually ill appearing and lay still because the pain is exacerbated by movement. The episode of pain lasts for several hours and is often aggravated by consumption of a fatty meal.

Gastroparesis (Answer A) is an autonomic complication in patients with diabetes and it is associated with nausea and early satiety. Affected patients are unlikely to present with abdominal pain.

Inflammatory bowel disease, including ulcerative colitis (Answer C), occurs at a higher incidence in patients with other autoimmune conditions. Like pancreatitis, ulcerative colitis can present with acute abdominal pain and signs of inflammation or infection. Usually there is a history of bloody diarrhea, or at least very frequent stools with mucus, which would not be the case in acute pancreatitis. The classic rash associated with ulcerative colitis is pyoderma gangrenosa, an ulcerating rash that tends to spread slowly.

##### ANSWER: E) Need for insulin therapy

The Look AHEAD trial recruited more than 5000 overweight or obese persons with type 2 diabetes and randomly assigned them to either intensive lifestyle changes (goal of achieving weight loss >7% by aiming for a calorie goal of 1200 to 1800 kcal per day and engaging in 175 minutes of moderate-intensity physical activity per week) or a control program of support and education about lifestyle changes. The study aim was to detect the impact of lifestyle intervention on cardiovascular events using a composite score. The composite score included death of cardiovascular causes, nonfatal myocardial infarction, nonfatal stroke, and hospitalization for angina. Both cohorts experienced weight loss; the average differential weight loss between the 2 cohorts was 4% over the course of the study and 2.5% at the end of the study. Yet, there was no significant reduction in cardiovascular events or mortality in the intervention group compared with outcomes in the control group (thus, Answers A, B, and C are incorrect). The intervention cohort did experience significant improvements in urinary incontinence, sleep apnea, depression, mobility, and physical functioning. A decreased need for glucose-lowering therapy was also observed in the intervention cohort (thus, Answers E is correct). Impact on diabetes-related complications was not studied (thus, Answers D is incorrect).

Despite the lack of a significant reduction in cardiovascular events in the intervention group, many reassuring findings in this study support continued encouragement of patients to pursue a healthful lifestyle. The control group that participated in educational sessions about lifestyle changes did achieve a weight loss of 3.5% by the end of the study. This and statin use have been considered factors in the overall low number of events observed in both cohorts. No details were provided regarding the weekly activity in which the study participants engaged.

102 Hypertriglyceridemia is associated with increased cardiovascular risk, although causality remains unclear. Triglyceride levels above 1000 mg/dL (>11.3 mmol/L) increase the risk of pancreatitis and should be aggressively treated with dietary and lifestyle interventions and usually concomitant pharmacologic therapy with fibrates, niacin, or fish oil.

Patients with diabetes mellitus who have hypertriglyceridemia should be screened for potentially treatable secondary causes such as hypothyroidism; nephrotic syndrome; obesity; alcohol use; and medication use including estrogens, glucocorticoids, cyclosporine, cyclophosphamide, noncardioselective  $ \beta $-adrenergic blockers, tamoxifen, phenothiazines, isotretinoin, and antiretroviral agents.

In patients with modest hypertriglyceridemia, the treatment focus should be nonpharmacologic and include weight loss in obese patients; regular aerobic exercise; and restriction of carbohydrates, especially food high in sugars. Among patients with diabetes, improved glycemic control effectively lowers triglyceride levels. In general, antihyperglycemic agents lower triglyceride levels. Insulin promotes the synthesis of lipoprotein lipase, which hydrolyzes triglycerides to free fatty acids. Intravenous insulin can effectively lower markedly elevated triglycerides. However, lowering triglycerides with prandial insulin has not been shown to reduce cardiovascular risk (thus, Answer E is incorrect).

Several cholesterol-lowering agents effectively lower triglycerides, including brates (18%-45%), niacin (17%-26%), and statins (5%-30%). High-dosage atorvastatin and rosuvastatin have the greatest triglyceride-lowering effect in the statin class. Niacin also raises HDL-cholesterol levels and has a modest LDL-lowering effect, but it has never been shown in monotherapy trials to reduce cardiovascular events rates or mortality among patients with diabetes (thus, Answer D is incorrect). Pharmacologic intervention of isolated hypertriglyceridemia with agents such as fibrates has not been shown to reduce cardiovascular event rates (thus, Answer B is incorrect). The Veterans Affairs High-Density Lipoprotein Intervention Trial (VA-HIT) showed a significant reduction in cardiovascular events among patients treated with gemfibrozil whose primary lipid abnormality was a low HDL-cholesterol level. Treated patients had a 6% increase in HDL cholesterol and a 31% lowering of triglycerides. In the Fenofibrate Intervention and Event Lowering in Diabetes (FIELD) trial, patients older than 50 years who had diabetes mellitus and were not taking lipid-lowering agents were treated with fenofibrate or placebo. After 5 years, treated patients had nonsignificantly lower rates of coronary events, and a nonsignificantly higher rate of coronary death and all-cause mortality. Combination therapy with a statin and fibrate has not been shown to improve cardiovascular risk reduction when compared with treatment with a statin alone (thus, Answer A is incorrect).

In addition to hypertriglyceridemia, this patient's cardiovascular risk factors include hypertension and albuminuria. The American Heart Association and the American College of Cardiology cardiovascular risk calculators scores this patient's 10-year cardiovascular risk at 17.6%, well above the 7.5% risk cutoff recommended for initiation of statin therapy, even in individuals whose baseline LDL-cholesterol concentration is well below 100 mg/dL (2.59 mmol/L). This older patient with type 2 diabetes has developed clinically significant hyperglycemia and an altered mental status requiring hospitalization. She meets established criteria for the hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state (HHS) because she has severe hyperglycemia (plasma glucose >600 mg/dL [>33.30 mmol/L]), hyperosmolality (effective serum osmolality >320 mOsm/kg [>320 mmol/kg]), and dehydration without evidence of marked ketoacidosis. In HHS, the arterial pH is typically above 7.30, the serum bicarbonate is greater than 15 mEq/L (>15 mmol/L), and the anion gap is variable. If detected, only small amounts of ketones are present in the urine and serum. This differs from diabetic ketoacidosis in which hyperglycemia is associated with elevated serum and urinary ketones and metabolic acidosis. In contrast to what is observed in diabetic ketoacidosis, endogenous insulin levels in HHS are generally high enough to prevent lipolysis and the generation of ketone bodies but are not high enough to facilitate glucose uptake into insulin-sensitive tissues.

HHS is estimated to comprise approximately 1% of all diabetes-related hospital admissions, and it is most commonly precipitated by inadequate or omitted insulin dosing or an infectious process. However, HHS may also be associated with conditions such as cerebrovascular or cardiovascular events, pancreatitis, or the use of medications such as glucocorticoids or thiazide diuretics. Older persons with type 2 diabetes are at greatest risk for the development of HHS because they are more likely to become severely dehydrated due to immobilization or an impaired thirst mechanism. The identification and appropriate management of HHS is essential. Even in developed countries, the associated mortality rate is estimated to be between 5% and 20% and is largely attributable to underlying precipitating conditions.

Individuals with HHS are very dehydrated, with estimated total water deficits at presentation typically between 8 and 10 L. Fluid repletion alone will greatly reduce hyperglycemia and reduce insulin resistance, and it represents a critical component of care. As is the case with this patient, the measured serum sodium is often low; however, this is misleading because it is due to the hyperglycemia-induced osmotic flux of water from the intracellular space to the extracellular space. This patient is in fact hypernatremic when the measured sodium level is corrected for the plasma glucose. Thus, the administration of hypertonic saline (Answer A) is not indicated and, in fact, might worsen her hyperosmolarity and hypernatremia. Initial fluid therapy in HHS should include isotonic saline administered at a rate of 15 to 20 mL/kg (or 1 to 1.5 L) over the first hour. Thereafter, hyponatremic patients should be administered isotonic saline at a rate of 250 to 500 mL/h, while patients with normal to high sodium levels should receive 0.45% saline at that rate. Administration of 0.45% saline at this time (Answers D and E) does not represent appropriate initial fluid resuscitation. Although not highlighted in this case, patients with HHS often have an initially high potassium level due to the shift of potassium into the extracellular space but are in fact total-body potassium depleted. Potassium replacement should begin when the serum potassium level falls below the upper limit of the normal range and should generally be maintained between 4 and 5 mEq/L (4 and 5 mmol/L).

HHS should also be managed via a low-dosage insulin infusion protocol. Administration of an initial 0.1 unit/kg body weight intravenous insulin bolus followed by 0.1 unit/kg per hour continuous insulin infusion is recommended; alternatively, administration of a continuous intravenous insulin infusion at a rate of 0.14 unit/kg body weight per hour is also appropriate. Use of such low-dosage insulin protocols generally decreases plasma glucose by 50 to 75 mg/dL per hour (2.78 to 4.16 mmol/L). Administration of isotonic saline now and an intravenous insulin infusion at 12 units per hour (Answer B) would adequately address this patient's immediate requirements. An intravenous insulin infusion of 4 units per hour (Answers C and E) would most likely not result in adequate glucose lowering. The primary goal of glycemic therapy is the maintenance of plasma glucose between 250 and 300 mg/dL (13.88 and 16.65 mmol/L) until the HHS is resolved. This is accomplished through adjustment of the rate of insulin infusion and the administration of dextrose. When the plasma glucose concentration reaches 300 mg/dL (16.65 mmol/L), the intravenous insulin infusion rate should be reduced to 0.02 to 0.05 units/kg per hour and dextrose should be added to the intravenous fluids. HHS is considered resolved when serum osmolality and mental status have returned to normal.
