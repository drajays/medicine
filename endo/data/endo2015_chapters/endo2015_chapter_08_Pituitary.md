### Answer 15: E) Improvement in diabetes mellitus

## 15 ANSWER: E) Improvement in diabetes mellitus

This patient has persistent acromegaly that was partially resistant to somatostatin analogue therapy after transsphenoidal surgery. Therefore, additional therapy is indicated and pegvisomant is a reasonable next therapeutic option for him. Given that this patient has diabetes, pegvisomant is especially appropriate medical therapy. Although the lowering of GH levels with somatostatin analogue therapy should improve insulin resistance in patients with acromegaly, these medications also suppress insulin levels. Thus, glucose intolerance and diabetes may not improve with somatostatin analogues. Pegvisomant, however, has been shown to improve insulin sensitivity and lower hemoglobin  $ A_{1c} $ levels in patients with acromegaly (thus, Answer E is correct).

Pegvisomant therapy is begun at a dosage of 10 mg daily and is titrated to achieve a normal IGF-1 concentration. In a small percentage of patients (2%-3%), liver enzymes rise on pegvisomant therapy. This tends to occur in the first 6 months of therapy and is usually transient. It is recommended that liver enzymes be monitored monthly after pegvisomant is initiated. If they rise to more than 3 times the upper normal limit of the reference range, pegvisomant therapy should be held. Therefore, monitoring liver enzymes regularly, especially in the first 6 months is necessary, but the development of abnormalities is unlikely (thus, Answer A is incorrect). If pegvisomant is held because of elevated liver enzymes, a re-challenge can be done if the liver enzymes normalize off therapy. Rarely do persistent enzyme elevations limit the use of pegvisomant.

Pituitary tumor size must also be monitored during pegvisomant therapy. However, the risk of pituitary tumor enlargement is small, roughly 3% over a 5-year period (thus, Answer B is incorrect). Pegvisomant does not

accelerate pituitary tumor growth. In patients taking pegvisomant, GH-secreting tumors seem to enlarge at the previous growth rate. Patients at higher risk for tumor enlargement are those whose tumors have been shown to enlarging before therapy, those who had significant shrinkage on somatostatin analogue therapy that is withdrawal or those who have not had radiotherapy. Although not necessary in this patient, if radiotherapy had been performed it would be expected to have reduced the chance of tumor enlargement on pegvisomant.

Tachyphylaxis to pegvisomant is not reported (thus, Answer D is incorrect).

Pegvisomant does not act on pituitary tumor GH secretion, but rather treats acromegaly by blocking peripheral receptors, thus lowering IGF-1 levels. On pegvisomant, GH levels remain the same or rise somewhat. Therefore, only IGF-1 levels, not GH levels, are monitored as a marker of pegvisomant's effectiveness (thus, Answer C is incorrect).

## 24 ANSWER: E) Postpone surgery and begin bromocriptine, 2.5 mg daily

This patient presents with a macroprolactinoma and associated vision loss. The initial treatment of choice for such patients is a dopamine agonist (Answer E). Either bromocriptine as in Answer E or cabergoline, which was not given as a therapeutic option, is an appropriate choice for dopamine agonist therapy. A trial of dopamine agonist for a patient with a clear diagnosis of prolactinoma is warranted before surgery (thus, Answers A, B, and C are incorrect). Dopamine agonists can rapidly shrink the tumor and improve vision. Surgery is reserved for scenarios when patients have severe, rapid vision loss or when pituitary apoplexy is unresponsive to conservative management. Dopamine agonists normalize prolactin levels in about 80% of men with macroprolactinomas and induce clinically significant pituitary tumor shrinkage in up to 90%. Vision impairment often improves within days of initiating dopamine agonist therapy. Prolactinomas, particularly in men, can grow very large before they are recognized, such as the tumor described in this vignette. Prolactinomas often have a characteristic, very invasive appearance on MRI.

High-dosage dexamethasone (Answer B) does not shrink macroprolactinomas and there is no indication for its use in the treatment of secondary adrenal insufficiency given this patient's normal cortisol level. Octreotide (Answer D) is also not indicated because he has a low GH level and no evidence of GH hypersecretion. Octreotide can shrink GH-secreting tumors, but it rarely achieves this rapidly enough to improve vision, so initial management of GH-secreting pituitary tumors, especially those associated with vision loss, is surgery. Radiotherapy after surgery (Answer C) is reserved for patients with known recurrence not amenable to surgical removal or a hormone-secreting tumor that cannot be treated medically.

## 35 ANSWER: E) No additional therapy now

This young man presented with gigantism, which is defined as acromegaly due to a GH-secreting pituitary tumor that presents in a patient younger than age 18 years. Postoperatively, his IGF-1 level has been falling, although it is not yet normal 2 months after surgery. His random GH level is low, but this alone is not used to distinguish remission from disease persistence. Only a random GH level that is very low, and less than the glucose-suppressed GH cutoff expected for remission, could be used to support a remission. Given his decreasing IGF-1 level and other aspects of his surgical and postoperative course, this patient is most likely in remission. IGF-1 levels often continue to fall during the first 3 months after successful resection of GH-secreting pituitary tumors, and some patients who achieve remission do not have a normal IGF-1 level until even longer after surgery. In a patient with a decreasing IGF-1 level and a GH level suggestive of remission, continued monitoring of IGF-1 and GH should be instituted and additional treatment of acromegaly should not be started too early after surgery. IGF-1 levels fall with age after puberty, so the upper normal limit of IGF-1 will fall every year for this young man. If he is truly in remission, this will be proven with continued follow-up. In another month (3 months postoperatively), IGF-1 and glucose-suppressed GH should be measured again. His glucose-suppressed GH level obtained 3 months after surgery should be less than 0.5 ng/mL (0.5 μg/L) when measured with most commercially available GH assays. Even without certainty about his disease status now, his clinical course should be followed without intervention for the time being (thus, Answer E is correct and Answers A, B, C, and D are incorrect).

A number of pitfalls exist when interpreting GH and IGF-1 levels both for diagnosis and determining remission in patients with acromegaly. Discrepant test results, with one hormone being elevated and the other being normal, is fairly common and can make determination of disease status difficult. In the diagnostic evaluation for acromegaly or in the evaluation of postoperative remission in a patient with known acromegaly, it is important to consider other conditions that can lead to discrepant GH and IGF-1 results. IGF-1 levels are only rarely elevated in conditions other than acromegaly, but they can be high in the setting of puberty, pregnancy, or hyperthyroidism. IGF-1 levels can be low in the context of hypothyroidism, malnutrition, poorly controlled type 1 diabetes mellitus, liver failure, renal failure, and oral estrogen use. GH can be elevated and fail to suppress normally in the absence of an acromegaly diagnosis in patients with liver disease, renal insufficiency, malnutrition, anorexia nervosa, or hyperthyroidism, as well as during adolescence or in patients taking oral estrogen.

47 ANSWER: E) The petrosal sinus sampling study

This patient has clear clinical and biochemical evidence of Cushing syndrome and a high ACTH level, supporting a diagnosis of ACTH-dependent Cushing syndrome. The next step in the evaluation is to distinguish between ACTH-dependent Cushing syndrome (Cushing disease) due to an ACTH-secreting pituitary tumor vs an ectopic source of ACTH hypersecretion. This patient underwent dexamethasone suppression testing to help differentiate among the causes. This patient failed to suppress serum cortisol (50%). Suppression of either cortisol levels is a common condition that is not well-classified by the patient. The patient is also a patient who has been农家-skilled with other health-related conditions, such as the use of anticoagulants, and other medical methods.

Petrosal sinus sampling is susceptible to a number of pitfalls. Importantly, for accurate results, hypercortisolemia must be present when inferior petrosal sinus sampling is done. Hypercortisolism in ectopic ACTH syndrome suppresses normal pituitary corticotrophs; thus, administration of corticotropin-releasing hormone does not stimulate ACTH secretion during the sampling procedure. In the presence of eucortisolemia, however, endogenous ACTH production from normal pituitary corticotrophs may no longer be suppressed and stimulation can occur. In this patient, ketoconazole therapy had normalized his cortisol excess, which therefore led to a false-positive result after corticotropin-releasing hormone stimulation (Answer E). The lack of central-peripheral gradient at baseline sampling might be suspicious in this patient, but the stimulation after corticotropin-releasing hormone would be the criterion used to determine whether a positive central-to-peripheral stimulation exists. Guidelines for interpretation of the ACTH gradient obtained from inferior petrosal sinus sampling state that a central-to-peripheral gradient greater than 2.0 at baseline or greater than 3.0 after corticotropin-releasing hormone stimulation is consistent with a pituitary source of Cushing disease. Were the inferior petrosal sinus sampling to have been done properly without pretreatment with ketoconazole, the results would have yielded the little or no central-to-peripheral ACTH gradient, consistent with ectopic ACTH syndrome.

## 105 ANSWER: E) Reassure the patient; no further evaluation is necessary

This patient's MRI demonstrates an empty sella. Empty sella is a radiographic diagnosis in which the subarachnoid space extends within the sella, such that cerebrospinal fluid is seen within the sella and the pituitary tissue is often flattened. It is a radiologic description and not a clinical diagnosis. In many cases, the empty sella appearance develops after known pituitary disease has enlarged the sella and/or altered the size and position of the pituitary gland. This is termed secondary empty sella and in adults it has been seen in association with a pituitary adenoma (including spontaneous infarction or surgical, medical, or radiation therapy for the adenoma). In addition, other pathologic processes involving the sellar region, such as Sheehan syndrome, Langerhans cell histiocytosis, spontaneous enlargement and rupture of a cyst, or granulomatous diseases affecting the pituitary, have been associated with empty sella. Empty sella not associated with one of the listed etiologies is termed primary empty sella. In adults, primary empty sella has been associated with congenital deficiency of the sellar diaphragm and benign intracranial hypertension (pseudotumor cerebri). Other risk factors for the development of an empty sella are pregnancy, obesity, and a previous autoimmune process involving the pituitary gland. Some patients with what appears to be primary empty sella may have had a pituitary disease that went undiagnosed.

Finding an empty sella on MRI is common and occurs more often in female patients. Some have suggested a relationship to previous pregnancies, with the enlarging and then shrinking of the pituitary in the postpartum period predisposing to the herniation of the sellar diaphragm into the sella and cerebrospinal fluid.

Headaches and a variety of vision disturbances have been reported in about one-third of patients with empty sella. Although previously considered very unlikely, hypopituitarism has more recently been reported in up to 40% to 50% of patients with empty sella. Hyperprolactinemia seems to be the most commonly associated endocrine abnormality. Patients with an empty sella observed on imaging should have basic testing of pituitary function, including measurement of prolactin, thyroid hormone, and morning cortisol. The chance of an endocrine abnormality is less likely in patients without clinical signs or symptoms in whom the empty sella was noted incidentally on a scan done for another reason. The need for any additional testing for hypopituitarism should be directed on the basis of the history and examination findings. Therefore, the chance of hypopituitarism in this patient is low and she should be reassured; no further evaluation is indicated (Answer E). More testing might be necessary in a different clinical scenario if a patient with empty sella had a history of a pituitary tumor or other sella lesion that may have secondarily led to the empty sella.

Given that the patient in this vignette has no clinical evidence of hormone hypersecretion and no visible tumor on MRI, there is no indication to assess her for a hormone-secreting tumor. Because she is 54 years old, whether or not her gonadotropins are elevated is not clinically relevant (thus, Answer A is incorrect). There is no evidence that incidentally noted empty sella on MRI progresses over time. Therefore, following this radiographic abnormality with serial imaging studies (Answer B) is unnecessary. This patient has hyperprolactinemia, which could be associated with the empty sella, but also could be due to paroxetine therapy. There is no need to evaluate her further for a prolactinoma because no lesion is visible. It is unlikely that the hook effect is occurring, and measuring her prolactin concentration with serial dilutions (Answer C) is incorrect. Visual field testing (Answer D) is not clinically indicated because there is no mass in her sella.

This patient's pituitary MRI demonstrates marked thickening of the pituitary stalk. This is an imaging finding that can be seen in diabetes insipidus due to antidiuretic hormone deficiency (Answer B). Pituitary MRI findings are variable in the context of diabetes insipidus and may be normal or demonstrate an absent posterior pituitary, loss of the posterior pituitary bright spot, or thickening of the pituitary stalk. In a review of 42 adolescents presenting with pituitary stalk thickening on MRI, 16 (38%) also had diabetes insipidus. One-half of those with diabetes insipidus had neoplastic disease, whereas none of the patients with only pituitary stalk thickening (and no diabetes insipidus) had neoplastic disease. In this patient, the pituitary abnormality was due to metastatic disease from her previous breast cancer, and pituitary imaging 6 months later demonstrated a clear seller and suprasellar mass that was confirmed to be metastatic disease.

The pituitary gland is an uncommon site for metastases, as metastatic disease accounts for only 1% to 2% of sellar masses. Although neoplasms from almost every tissue have been reported to metastasize to the pituitary, the

most common primary tumors are breast or lung cancers. Metastatic deposits demonstrate a predilection for the posterior lobe of the pituitary, mainly because there is a direct arterial blood supply (via the inferior hypophyseal artery, which branches from the internal carotid artery) that facilitates hematogenous spread. In a review of more than 200 cases, the posterior lobe was involved alone or with the anterior pituitary in 85% of cases, whereas only the anterior lobe was affected in 15%. Therefore, metastatic deposits in the anterior lobe are usually due to contiguous spread from the posterior lobe.

The patient's imaging findings and previous history of breast cancer make diabetes insipidus the most likely initial endocrine dysfunction in this case. Therefore, all other answers (A, C, D, and E) are incorrect, although they may also develop with time as the metastatic disease progresses and the lesion enlarges. Indeed, in the review of 42 adolescents with pituitary stalk thickening and diabetes insipidus, 44% subsequently developed anterior pituitary dysfunction. In this case, pituitary MRI performed 6 months later demonstrated a clear sellar and suprasellar mass and the patient developed ACTH and TSH deficiencies.

Pituitary metastases are, however, often asymptomatic. In the aforementioned review, 40 of 200 patients (20%) were symptomatic, and, of these, diabetes insipidus was documented in 28 patients (70%) while only 6 patients (15%) had 1 or more anterior pituitary deficiencies.
