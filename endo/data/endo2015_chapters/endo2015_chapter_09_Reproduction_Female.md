The AURORA trial (A Study to Evaluate the Use of Rosuvastatin in Subjects on Regular Hemodialysis: An Assessment of Survival and Cardiovascular Events) evaluated rosuvastatin's effect on cardiovascular end points in patients receiving hemodialysis and found no benefit compared with observations in the placebo group. The Study of Heart and Renal Protection (SHARP) randomly assigned patients with chronic kidney disease 3 through 7 to placebo or simvastatin plus ezetimibe. Although there was evidence of benefit overall, the subgroup analysis suggested that this was mainly driven by the nondialysis group and the patients undergoing dialysis had no benefit. It is important to note that statins reduce the relative risk of cardiovascular disease to a similar extent in patients with predialysis chronic kidney disease as they do in patients without chronic kidney disease, but because patients with chronic kidney disease have a greater baseline risk, the absolute risk reduction with statin treatment is greater in patients with predialysis chronic kidney disease than in patients without chronic kidney disease.

Although several post hoc analyses of these trials have suggested potential benefit for various subgroups of patients receiving dialysis, conclusions from 3 major trials have not found a conclusive benefit for statin use in patients receiving dialysis. Statins are not routinely recommended; however, they are not contraindicated (thus, Answers D is incorrect). There are no guidelines for patients who are taking statins at the time of progression to dialysis; thus, the decision to continue or cease statin therapy should be made on a personalized basis. However, there is fairly good evidence that statin therapy improves cardiovascular survival after renal transplant and statin should be considered in that setting.

Distinguishing between polycystic ovary syndrome and functional hypothalamic amenorrhea (Answer C) can be difficult. This patient has amenorrhea in the setting of training for a half-marathon, suggesting that her increased exercise caused the amenorrhea. However, functional hypothalamic amenorrhea is caused by low energy availability, which suppresses GnRH pulse secretion. The decreased GnRH pulsatility results in decreased LH levels with a normal FSH level, such that FSH is higher than LH. The low LH does not stimulate androstenedione and testosterone production from theca cells, and estradiol is low because of the absence of androgen precursor for aromatization. In the vignette, the patient has an elevated LH-to-FSH ratio, an elevated testosterone concentration, and an estradiol concentration in normal range—this pattern of test results is not consistent with functional hypothalamic amenorrhea.

In addition to hypothalamic amenorrhea, other causes of irregular menses must be excluded to diagnose polycystic ovary syndrome. The patient has a normal FSH level, which excludes primary ovarian insufficiency, which was previously termed premature ovarian failure (Answer E). The prolactin and TSH levels are normal, which excludes hyperprolactinemia and thyroid disease. There is no pregnancy test reported, but if she were pregnant, her estradiol level would be higher and her FSH and LH would be suppressed, so pregnancy (Answer D) is an unlikely cause of her amenorrhea. Other causes of hyperandrogenism must be excluded by history or by laboratory testing. The long history of acne starting at puberty is not consistent with an androgen-secreting tumor. The normal 17-hydroxyprogesterone level rules out nonclassic congenital adrenal hyperplasia; and there is no evidence for Cushing syndrome on physical examination.

Hormonal contraception will not cause amenorrhea after it has been stopped. At the hormone dosages current available, “postpill amenorrhea” (Answer B) is unlikely. FSH begins to rise in the 7 days of the placebo week and follicles can grow in the short interval. Therefore, there is no prolonged suppression of the reproductive axis after stopping hormonal contraception. Taken together, other causes of irregular menses and hyperandrogenism have been ruled out and polycystic ovary syndrome is the correct diagnosis (Answer A).

### Answer 44: C) Clomiphene citrate Women with polycystic ovary syndrome

## 44 ANSWER: C) Clomiphene citrate Women with polycystic ovary syndrome

Women with polycystic ovary syndrome have infertility related to irregular menstrual cycles and anovulation. Clomiphene citrate (Answer C) is the best first-line medication to induce ovulation in this setting. Clomiphene citrate is a selective estrogen receptor modulator that inhibits estradiol's negative feedback at the hypothalamus, resulting in increased GnRH pulse frequency. The increased GnRH pulsatility stimulates LH and FSH, which then drive follicle growth and estradiol production. The improvement in pregnancy and live birth rate is significant when compared with outcomes using placebo.

Metformin (Answer B) is used off-label for polycystic ovary syndrome because it increases menstrual cycle frequency and pregnancy rate. However, the pregnancy rate is lower than with clomiphene citrate, and metformin does not improve the live birth rate. Therefore, it is not the treatment of choice for infertility. Further, the pregnancy rate using a combination of clomiphene citrate and metformin (Answer D) is not superior to clomiphene alone. In women who do not respond to treatment with clomiphene citrate, recombinant human FSH (Answer A) is the second-line treatment choice.

A recent meta-analysis demonstrated that letrozole, an aromatase inhibitor (Answer E), improves the pregnancy and live birth rate in women with polycystic ovary syndrome when compared with rates observed with clomiphene citrate. However, the quality of the evidence was deemed to be low and it was suggested that these results be regarded with caution. Therefore, an aromatase inhibitor is not yet the recommended treatment for ovulation induction in women with polycystic ovary syndrome until stronger evidence is available.

### Answer 60: E) Spironolactone and norethindrone-only hormonal contraception

## 60 ANSWER: E) Spironolactone and norethindrone-only hormonal contraception

60 The risk of stroke is increased in patients with migraines and visual aura compared with the risk in patients who have migraines without aura. A concerning visual aura includes a zigzag line that moves laterally in the visual field or an area of lost vision that spreads laterally surrounded by normal visual fields, as described in the vignette. The etiology of a visual aura may be ischemia, which could underlie the increased risk for stroke. Studies suggest that the risk of stroke is 2 to 4 times higher for women with migraines taking oral contraceptives than those without, but no studies have had sufficient numbers to examine women with aura vs those without aura. The risk of stroke is so low in women of reproductive age that migraines without aura are not a contraindication to hormonal contraception as long as a woman is healthy, younger than 35 years, and does not smoke. In contrast, migraines with aura at any age are a contraindication for combined estrogen and progestin hormonal contraception (Answer D).

In this vignette, the patient wants treatment for hirsutism and acne. While combined estrogen and progestin is the treatment of choice for acne and hirsutism, the therapy is contraindicated in this patient. Spironolactone and norethindrone-only contraception (Answer E) would be the best combination to treat her primary symptoms.

Spironolactone, used off-label for polycystic ovary syndrome, alleviates acne and hirsutism through blockade of androgen receptor in the skin. Progestin-only contraception, which does not exacerbate migraines, protects the uterus from unopposed endogenous estrogen exposure. It also prevents pregnancy, which is important when spironolactone is used because it causes ambiguous genitalia in male fetuses in animal models and is classified as a pregnancy category C drug.

Although progestin-only contraception in the form of a subcutaneous depot (medroxyprogesterone), pill (norethindrone), or coated intrauterine device (levonorgestrel) (Answer C) would all be acceptable alternatives for contraception and uterine protection, they do not address her primary concern when used alone. Similarly, monthly micronized progesterone (Answer A) protects the uterus from unopposed estrogen exposure, but does not address the cosmetic concerns or the possibility of pregnancy. Metformin (Answer B) can increase the number of ovulatory menstrual cycles in women with polycystic ovary syndrome, but it is not a good treatment for hirsutism or acne.

## 83 ANSWER: B) Add a hormonal contraceptive

83 The therapeutic goals for women with classic congenital adrenal hyperplasia are to maintain regular menses and alleviate symptoms of androgen excess while avoiding the complications of glucocorticoid replacement (eg, iatrogenic Cushing syndrome, dyslipidemia, glucose intolerance, hypertension, obesity, bone loss, and cardiovascular disease). This patient has no signs of adrenal insufficiency but does have signs of androgen excess including irregular menses, hirsutism, and acne, which are her main concerns. Her testosterone level is also elevated; therefore, additional treatment of androgen excess is needed. However, increasing the glucocorticoid dosage (Answer C) to suppress adrenal androgens is not the best choice for treating elevated testosterone in this patient who has no symptoms of adrenal insufficiency. Women with congenital adrenal hyperplasia can develop secondary ovarian androgen excess resulting in elevated testosterone despite appropriate glucocorticoid replacement. Hormonal contraception (Answer B) is the treatment of choice for hirsutism. It suppresses ovarian androgens and increases sex hormone-binding globulin, which decreases free testosterone. Hormonal contraception will also protect the uterus, preventing endometrial hyperplasia when menses are irregular. Hormonal contraception is therefore the best treatment strategy in this patient.

The goal of mineralocorticoid replacement with fludrocortisone is to suppress renin so that there is no hypovolemia driving ACTH secretion. In this patient, the renin activity is suppressed, blood pressure is normal, there are no orthostatic symptoms, and potassium is normal, which suggests that mineralocorticoids are not over-replaced or under-replaced. Therefore, there is no need to increase the fludrocortisone dosage (Answer A).

Glucocorticoid replacement for congenital adrenal hyperplasia can be given in many different forms. Hydrocortisone has the shortest half-life and is least likely to result in over-replacement. Although the short half-life warrants dosing 3 times per day, it is often tolerated very well when given twice daily in adults with congenital adrenal hyperplasia. Therefore, there is no need to increase the hydrocortisone dosage (Answer C). The glucocorticoid dosage should not be titrated to suppress the 17-hydroxyprogesterone into the normal range because the 17-hydroxyprogesterone levels tend to be much higher than androgen levels and normalization will result in over-treatment. Synthetic, long-acting glucocorticoids (Answer D) may suppress androgens better than hydrocortisone, particularly the increased androgens in the morning when ACTH peaks. However, these long-acting glucocorticoids are more likely to cause iatrogenic Cushing syndrome.

Spironolactone (Answer E), used off-label, does act as an antiandrogen and decreases the testosterone action at the receptor level. It can be used in women with congenital adrenal hyperplasia, but it must be paired with increased fludrocortisone because of its diuretic effect. Spironolactone can also result in ambiguous genitalia in a male infant, and it should therefore be used with contraception in female patients.

## 101 ANSWER: D) Switch to micronized progesterone, 200 mg Progesterone is necessary to protect the

101 Progesterone is necessary to protect the uterus from endometrial hyperplasia and cancer when estrogen is taken for menopausal symptoms in a woman with an intact uterus. Regimens using medroxyprogesterone acetate and micronized progesterone have been demonstrated to prevent endometrial hyperplasia in large studies. Micronized progesterone may be better tolerated and have a less adverse effect on lipids than medroxyprogesterone. Therefore, switching to micronized progesterone, 200 mg for 12 days of the month (Answer D), or 100 mg daily, would be the most appropriate next step.

The medroxyprogesterone acetate regimens that have been demonstrated to prevent endometrial hyperplasia include medroxyprogesterone acetate, 5 or 10 mg for 12 days of the month, or medroxyprogesterone acetate, 2.5 mg daily. There are not enough data to suggest that medroxyprogesterone acetate, 2.5 mg for 12 days of the month (Answer C), adequately prevents endometrial hyperplasia.

If a patient cannot take any type of progesterone by mouth, a levonorgestrel-coated intrauterine device could be considered. In meta-analyses, it has been demonstrated that the levonorgestrel-coated intrauterine device causes the endometrial lining to become atrophic and prevents endometrial hyperplasia. Intravaginal progesterone (Answer E) has also been shown to decrease the incidence of endometrial hyperplasia, but only in small studies. Therefore, it would not be the best treatment choice. In women who use intravaginal progesterone and in women who cannot take progesterone in any form, baseline and yearly endometrial biopsies are required to detect endometrial hyperplasia, with additional endometrial biopsies if there is new-onset vaginal bleeding.

Short-term (<5 years) hormone therapy is safe in a woman with no contraindications, such as personal or family history of breast cancer, thrombotic events, coronary artery disease, or endometrial cancer. This patient has no contraindications to hormone therapy and has had remarkable relief of her symptoms. Therefore, stopping estradiol (Answer A) would not be appropriate. Further, although venlafaxine (Answer B) has been demonstrated to alleviate hot flashes in menopause, it is not as effective as estradiol, and switching to venlafaxine would not be the most appropriate next treatment step.

### Answer 109: E) Erythrocytosis

109 ANSWER: E) Erythrocytosis

To achieve masculinization of a female-to-male transgender individual, testosterone is administered to target a normal male range of 300 to 1200 ng/dL (10.4 to 41.6 nmol/L), 1 week after testosterone administration if given intramuscularly, but levels less than 700 ng/dL (24.3 nmol/L) are recommended. Transgender individuals should be seen every 3 months during the first year of treatment, then every 6 to 12 months to monitor testosterone levels; the effectiveness of therapy; and potential adverse effects including erythrocytosis, weight gain, salt retention, lipid abnormalities, cystic acne, and psychological changes. The testosterone level in this individual is greater than 700 ng/dL (24.3 nmol/L) the day before administration of the next testosterone dose, suggesting that his testosterone levels are always higher than the recommended range. Testosterone replacement may cause erythrocytosis (Answer E). The transgender man in this vignette is also overweight and has acne, which might improve slightly with a lower testosterone dosage.

Few transgender men can afford to have a hysterectomy, salpingo-oophorectomy, and mastectomy. Therefore, clinical breast examinations, mammograms, and Papanicolaou testing should be performed at recommended intervals as indicated by the individual's risk group. Testosterone levels in the male range often result in amenorrhea. Female-to-male transgender patients are at risk for endometrial cancer based on aromatization of testosterone to estradiol (Answer D), and endometrial hyperplasia was described in 3 of 19 transgender patients treated with androgens. However, the endometrium is typically atrophic with high-dosage testosterone therapy when examined after hysterectomy and no cases of endometrial cancer have been reported. Therefore, the actual risk of endometrial cancer is unknown.

Liver function abnormalities (Answer A) should not increase with intramuscular testosterone administration, although liver function should be evaluated every 6 months to 1 year because transient elevation in liver enzymes is common.

Low bone mineral density (Answer C) is not a concern if testosterone is maintained in the male range.

Although male-to-female transgender individuals on estrogen therapy are at high risk for thromboembolic events (Answer B), female-to-male transgender individuals are not.
