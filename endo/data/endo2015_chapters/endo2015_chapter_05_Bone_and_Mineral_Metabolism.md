## 23 ANSWER: C) Denosumab

25 In addition to lifestyle changes, including weight-bearing exercise, avoidance of tobacco, and adequate calcium and vitamin D intake, pharmacologic treatment should be offered to this patient, as she is at increased risk for osteoporosis and fracture. Thus, providing reassurance that she needs no further treatment (Answer E) is inappropriate; treatment is warranted.

Aromatase inhibitors such as anastrozole or letrozole work by inhibiting the conversion of adrenal androgens into estrogens, which is the primary source of estrogen in postmenopausal women. Multiple trials have demonstrated aromatase inhibitors produce significant decreases in bone mineral density and increase fracture risk because estrogen deprivation. The greatest decline in bone density occurs in premenopausal women receiving both ovarian suppression therapy (GnRH agonist) and an aromatase inhibitor because of the rapid reduction in endogenous estrogen levels. GnRH agonist (Answer B) would not be appropriate for this patient since she is postmenopausal.

Denosumab (Answer C), a monoclonal antibody against RANKL, increases the mineral density in women undergoing treatment with aromatase inhibitors and is approved for the prevention of aromatase inhibitor–induced bone loss in postmenopausal women. Practical guidance provided by Hadji et al recommends that patients initiating aromatase inhibitors be advised regarding exercise, calcium and vitamin D supplementation, and base bone mineral density monitoring and that bone-directed therapy be initiated if the T score is less than -2.0 at either the hip or the spine or at least 2 fracture risk factors are detected. Patients with a T score greater than -2.0 and the hip or the spine should be followed up closely with sequential measurement of bone mineral density, and therapy

Tamoxifen (Answer A) has beneficial effects on bone mineral density in postmenopausal women, and several trials have compared tamoxifen with aromatase inhibitors and found an increase in fracture rates with aromatase inhibitors compared with fracture rates with tamoxifen. However, tamoxifen in combination with an aromatase inhibitor has not been studied in terms of bone health, nor would this combination be recognized as appropriate therapy for this patient at her stage of disease management.

Calcitonin (Answer D) is a weak antiresorptive agent approved for prevention of lumbar spine fractures in postmenopausal women. The efficacy of calcitonin, when used concurrently with an aromatase inhibitor, has not been studied. Calcitonin has not been shown to alter bone strength at the femoral neck and hence it is not approved for prevention of hip fracture.

### Answer 29: A) Elemental calcium, 2000 mg daily

## 29 ANSWER: A) Elemental calcium, 2000 mg daily

29 After bariatric surgery, the loss of bone mineral density documented on DXA scan is relatively common. In patient, some of the bone loss seen on DXA is artifact due to extreme changes in fat mass. DXA measurement is limited in the obese population because of the inability to discriminate between cortical and trabecular bone. Indeed studies done with quantitative CT tend to reveal stable bone mass, suggesting that the DXA results may be artifact. However, markers of bone turnover (both formation and resorption) increase markedly after weight-reduction surge and hence the potential for clinically significant bone loss is a possible adverse outcome with these procedures.

Bone loss associated with bariatric surgery is multifactorial. There is a strong association between the low weight loss and loss of bone mineral density in weight-bearing sites including the hip and femoral neck, while is postulated to be due to adaptive changes in skeletal unloading. At baseline, morbidly obese patients have low vitamin D levels because of reduced bioavailability from sequestration into fat tissue. Malabsorption of vitamin D is also found in patients with other nutrients, after bariatric surgery is well recognized. Vitamin D dosages needed to restore normal levels after bariatric surgery can be extremely large, perhaps up to 15,000 or 20,000 IU daily. Ergocalciferol (vitamin D₃) comes in a 50,000 IU preparation and is often used for high-dosage replacement.

This patient has secondary hyperparathyroidism with a normal serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D level. Thus, additional vitamin D (Answers B) is not needed. However, the urinary calcium excretion is exceedingly low and the secondary hyperparathyroidism is thus due to calcium deficiency. Calcium transporters are most prevalent in the duodenum; this is the area of best absorption of both dietary calcium and supplements. After Roux-en-Y bariatric surgery, the duodenum is bypassed. Current guidelines recommend up to 1500 mg of calcium daily a minimum of 3000 IU of vitamin D daily to avoid bone loss. In this patient with declining bone density and calmalabsorption, a higher dosage of calcium such as 2000 mg daily or more, may be needed (Answer A is correct).

Secondary hyperparathyroidism would not be treated with parathyroidectomy. PTH rise is in response to the calcium deficiency, not the primary problem. Calcium receptor mimetics such as cinacalcet can reduce PTH levels but do not address the inherent process causing the problem. Indeed, calcium receptor mimetics reduce calcium levels and could cause clinical hypocalcemia in this patient with circulating calcium levels already at the lower end of the normal range (thus, Answer D is incorrect).

One should not institute antiresorptive therapy with alendronate (Answer C) or any other bisphosphonate the presence of either calcium or vitamin D deficiency.

## 36 ANSWER: C) Measure transferrin saturation and ferritin concentration

36 ANSWER: Men with osteoporosis should be evaluated for secondary causes because in almost half of cases, a modern condition causing bone loss is identified. Although this patient reports fatigue and has a history of hypothyroidism, the is on levothyroxine replacement, and his serum TSH and free T₄ concentrations are normal. In patients with hemochromatosis (as is the case here), secondary hypothyroidism is possible and the TSH level may be misleadingly low. With a normal free T₄ level, the diagnosis of secondary hypothyroidism is excluded. Moreover, secondary hypothyroidism is uncommon and without signs of hypothyroidism on examination (bradycardia, delayed relaxation phase of reflexes, etc), this diagnosis would be highly unlikely. Measuring free T₄ levels (Answer A) would probably not provide additional diagnostic information and would not explain osteoporosis, hypogonadism, and new-onset diabetes. Likewise, measuring free testosterone (Answer D) would not be appropriate to evaluate for an adenoma, mass, and infiltrative causes of central hypogonadism with resultant osteoporosis. Involvement of only the pituitary would not explain the new-onset diabetes.

Hemochromatosis is a hereditary disorder that results in iron deposition in multiple organs, including the endocrine glands. Diabetes mellitus, both primary and secondary hypogonadism, secondary hypothyroidism, and hepatic dysfunction can all result from iron overload. In fact, this patient has an elevated fasting glucose concentration, consistent with β-cell destruction by iron overload. Iron accumulation also occurs in the pituitary, which causes secondary hypogonadism, reflected by low FSH and LH levels, as observed in this patient. Additionally, there are reported cases of testicular failure due to gonadal iron deposition. The hypogonadism results from hemochromatosis most likely contributes to osteoporosis, and this patient should receive testosterone replacement, at least until testicular function may be restored. Depending on the severity and duration of untreated hemochromatosis, pituitary function may return with iron-depletion strategies. Interestingly, the rates of osteoporosis and fractures are higher than would be predicted by hypogonadism alone, and can even occur in the absence of hypogonadism. Patients with hemochromatosis have a higher incidence of osteopenia and osteoporosis as well as an increased fracture rate. Phlebotomy and reduction of iron accumulation can improve bone mineral density, and proper diagnosis is crucial. A fasting transferrin saturation and ferritin concentration (Answer C) should be assessed.

## 74 ANSWER: C) PTHrP

The markedly elevated carbohydrate antigen 19-9 (CA19-9) level is highly suggestive of cholangiocarcinoma, which was confirmed by FNAB of the patient's scapular mass. Cholangiocarcinoma is a rare epithelial tumor of the biliary tree, often locally advanced at the time of initial presentation and associated with high mortality. It metastasizes via the lymphatic system and less commonly spreads to bone with a predilection for the axial skeleton.

The hypercalcemia seen in cholangiocarcinoma is due to either of 2 mechanisms: tumor secretion of PTHrP or the local destruction and release of calcium from bone (which is not an answer option in this vignette). Most commonly, and as described in multiple case reports, hypercalcemia is associated with cholangiocarcinoma due to tumor PTHrP production, as was seen in this patient (thus, Answer C is correct).

PTHrP shares 70% homology with the first 13 amino acids of the aminoterminal end of PTH. Binding of PTHrP to the PTH 1 receptor simulates the major effects including osteoblastic bone resorption and distal tubular calcium reabsorption, which impairs calcium excretion but blunts proximal tubular reabsorption of phosphorus. Beyond the first 13 amino acids, the protein is unique and shows no further sequence homology with PTH. PTHrP additionally enhances malignant cell growth and survival via the secretion of tumor-activating transforming growth factor- $ \beta $ that stimulates local cancer cells.

Patients with PTHrP-mediated hypercalcemia have lower 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D₃ levels than patients with primary hyperparathyroidism given less stimulation of 1α-hydroxylase activity. PTHrP therefore would not be expected to increase intestinal absorption of calcium. The increased level of alkaline phosphatase in this patient could be the result of direct bony involvement by the tumor, PTHrP-stimulated bone turnover, or a combination of the two.

24,25-Dihydroxyvitamin D (Answer A) is an inactive metabolite of vitamin D and would not cause hypercalcemia. Ectopic PTH (Answer B) is considerably less common than PTHrP in all types of malignancy. Local factors, such as PTHrP and transforming growth factor-β, can mediate osteoclast activity and bone resorption in malignancy. Elevated fibroblast growth factor 23 levels are associated with tumor-induced osteomalacia, which is an acquired disorder of renal phosphate wasting that typically occurs with mesenchymal tumors. Tumor-induced osteomalacia is not associated with hypercalcemia (thus, Answer D is incorrect). Hypercortisolism (Answer E) is associated with bone loss for a number of reasons including diminished osteoblast activity, enhanced renal calcium excretion, and secondary hyperparathyroidism. However, hypercalcemia is not a finding in hypercortisolism. Cholangiocarcinomas have not been reported to make ACTH, ectopic production of which is seen in malignancies of the lung and other tissues. Moreover, there is no evidence of hyperpigmentation in this patient, although in malignancy-associated ectopic ACTH and Cushing syndrome, the time course may be so rapid that dermal responses to the high ACTH are not evident.

82 Parathyroid carcinoma is a rare cause of primary hyperparathyroidism that is seen in less than 1% to 2% of cases. ## 94 ANSWER: B) Intravenous zoledronic acid

Vertebral compression fractures are a risk factor for subsequent vertebral fractures, as well as other osteoporotic fractures. They are also associated with significant morbidity, including chronic back pain, restricted lung function, reduced mobility, and decreased quality of life. Unfortunately, most patients with fragility fractures are not treated for osteoporosis, despite evidence that treatment is beneficial.

Patients who present with vertebral fractures should be worked up for secondary causes of osteoporosis. This patient had a full secondary osteoporosis evaluation, which was unrevealing. Of interest, there is a potential connection between proton-pump inhibitors and bone loss, such as is seen in this patient. Although the exact mechanism remains unproven, the increase in gastric pH reduces the absorption of calcium and thereby results in one loss when these medications are used long term.

This vignette presents a common scenario in which an asymptomatic vertebral compression fracture is noted on imaging obtained for other reasons. It is the clinician's role to recognize these events as skeletal failure that requires prompt evaluation and treatment. This patient's atraumatic spinal compression fracture confirms the diagnosis of osteoporosis, despite the fact that his bone mineral density does not meet the T-score definition of osteoporosis (-2.5 or less). The National Osteoporosis Foundation recommends the following: (1) initiating pharmacologic treatment in patients with hip or vertebral fractures (clinical or asymptomatic) regardless of T-score; (2) initiating therapy in patients with T scores of -2.5 or less at the femoral neck, total hip, or lumbar spine by OXA after appropriate evaluation; and (3) initiating therapy in postmenopausal women and men 50 years or older who have low bone mineral density (T score between -1.0 and -2.5 [osteopenia]) at the femoral neck, total hip, or lumbar spine by DXA and 10-year probabilities of hip fracture or major osteoporotic fracture of ≥3% and ≥20%, respectively (based on the US-adapted WHO absolute fracture risk model [FRAX; www.NOF.org and www.shefac.uk/FRAX]). Although the FRAX score in this patient is under the threshold for treatment, as it only accounts for femoral neck T score, on the basis of his recent vertebral fracture, treatment is warranted.

Oral bisphosphonates (such as alendronate and risedronate) and zoledronic acid are good options for first-line therapy because they reduce the risk of hip and spine fracture. Although oral alendronate (Answer A) would be a treatment option in this patient, given his recent duodenal ulcer and esophageal reflux disease, it may not be the best choice. Gastroesophageal disease is not an absolute contraindication to use of oral bisphosphonates, but caution should be used in patient with active upper gastrointestinal problems such as Barrett esophagus, dysphagia, other esophageal diseases, gastritis, duodenitis, or ulcers. Intravenous zoledronic acid (Answer B) confers no gastrointestinal risk for this patient and therefore would be the preferred choice.

Calcitonin (Answer D) reduces risk of spine fracture and may offer analgesic effects in some patients with back pain related to acute fracture; however, it is less potent than other available options and this patient is asymptomatic. Although results from randomized, placebo-controlled trials have suggested little or no benefit in patients with acute vertebral compression fracture, kyphoplasty (Answer C) (the installation of bone cement after inflation of the vertebral body) has been used in patients with severe, incapacitating pain related to the fracture expansion of the vertebral body. Adding calcium (Answer E) would not be appropriate as the sole intervention, as calcium alone does not reduce hip fracture risk. In addition, he is already consuming at least 600 mg of calcium daily in his diet, as well as 500 mg daily in his multivitamin.

95 A substantial increase in the use of radiocontrast-enhanced imaging studies has occurred over the past 2 decades (particularly CT and coronary angiography). Thyroid dysfunction may result as a consequence of sudden exposure to high iodide levels, and both hyperthyroidism and hypothyroidism may occur as a consequence of using iodinated contrast medium (ICM). Radiocontrast agents vary with respect to their iodine content. The usual iodine load in coronary angiography is 100 mL of ICM, conveying 13,500 mcg of free iodide and 30 to 35 g of bound iodine. If coronary angiography is associated with percutaneous coronary intervention, up to 250 mL may be used, resulting in an iodine load of 75 to 87.5 g. The recommended daily intake of iodine for nonpregnant adults is only 150 mcg. However, supraphysiologic doses are well tolerated and the approximate levels above which significant adverse effects are likely to occur in the healthy population are actually much higher. The US Institute of Medicine reports the tolerable upper limit of iodine as 1100 mcg daily.

In individuals with preexisting autoimmunity, supraphysiologic levels of iodine may lead to iodine-induced hyperthyroidism (Jod-Basedow phenomenon). Iodine overload is accompanied by the Wolff-Chaikoff effect, which inhibits iodine organization and thereby reduces formation and release of thyroid hormones. After 8 to 10 days, there usually is an escape phenomenon secondary to low inorganic iodine concentration as a consequence of downregulation of the sodium-iodide symporter on the basolateral membrane of follicular thyroid cells. The presence of autonomous thyroid function permits the synthesis and release of excess quantities of thyroid hormones, resulting in iodine-induced thyrotoxicosis. Risk factors for this condition include Graves disease, nontoxic diffuse or multinodular goiter with thyroid autonomy, age older than 65 years, and residing in an iodine-deficient area. The link between exposure to ICM and new thyroid dysfunction is more common than was previously thought. Iodine-induced thyrotoxicosis cannot easily be differentiated from other forms of thyrotoxicosis.

Failure to escape from the Wolff-Chaikoff effect results in iodine-induced hypothyroidism. This may occur in apparently healthy individuals, especially in neonates and infants; in patients older than 65 years; or in patients

with chronic nonthyroidal illness such as cystic fibrosis, chronic dialysis, thalassemia major, or anorexia nervosa. It may also occur in patients with underlying thyroid disease including euthyroid Graves disease previously treated with surgery, in patients treated with antithyroid drugs or radioiodine, in patients with Hashimoto thyroiditis, and in euthyroid patients with a history of thyroiditis. Furthermore, patients treated with other goitrogens including lithium, phenytoin, carbamazepine, and rifampicin are at risk of ICM-induced hypothyroidism.

The patient described in this vignette has a number of risk factors for ICM-induced thyrotoxicosis including a family history of thyroid dysfunction and the presence of a multinodular goiter. Prophylactic measures have been proposed for patients at high risk, especially if the serum TSH is undetectable. This patient's diabetes is complicated by end-stage renal disease requiring the relatively recent initiation of renal replacement therapy. Subsequent to the initiation of peritoneal dialysis he has had persistent elevation in his self-monitored blood glucose values, resulting in recent increases in his prescribed insulin doses. Although the patient was not reported to have had hypoglycemia at home, he has experienced an episode of severe hypoglycemia on this occasion requiring the intervention of emergency medical personnel.

In this situation, it is critical to note that the patient is using an icodextrin-containing peritoneal dialysate solution. Icodextrin is commonly used as an osmotic agent in such solutions, and it is primarily metabolized to the sugar maltose. Many glucose meters and test strips do not measure glucose via glucose-specific methodologies. These devices cannot distinguish glucose from xylose, maltose, or galactose, and they may provide falsely elevated readings in the presence of such sugars. Indeed, numerous reports document individuals using icodextrin-containing dialysate being prescribed excessive insulin doses in response to erroneous glucose meter data. In some cases, this has resulted in severe hypoglycemia, coma, and even death. Thus, inaccurate glucose meter data due to the patient's dialysate type (Answer A) is most likely the primary contributor to his hypoglycemia. Patients prescribed this type of dialysate should always use glucose-specific monitors and test strips. A list of devices (glucose-specific vs non-glucose-specific) is available at www.glucosesafety.com. Also important to note is that in patients with clinically significant renal dysfunction, icodextrin and maltose metabolites may not be cleared for 2 or more weeks after icodextrin is discontinued.

Compared with individuals with normal renal function, patients with chronic kidney disease are at increased risk for hypoglycemia. In patients with diabetes and end-stage renal disease, multiple factors contribute to this increased risk. ### Answer 100: B) Measurement of 25-hydroxyvitamin D

## 100 ANSWER: B) Measurement of 25-hydroxyvitamin D

Obesity is associated with vitamin D deficiency due to reduced bioavailability from sequestration in fat. Patients who undergo bariatric surgery are at increased risk for malabsorption of vitamin D, leading to further nutritional deficits because of the loss of sufficient gastrointestinal absorptive surface. Deficiency in 25-hydroxyvitamin D results in decreased calcium and phosphorous absorption from the gut and increased PTH, which leads to increased bone resorption to maintain serum calcium in the normal range. Severe and prolonged vitamin D deficiency results in osteomalacia or a mineralization defect in bone where osteoid remains undermineralized, soft, and vulnerable to fracture. Therefore, measurement of 25-hydroxyvitamin D (Answer B) is the best diagnostic step now.

Osteomalacia is characterized by deficient bone mineralization, and in combination with hyperparathyroidism, it can lead to an increase in bone loss and fractures. Affected patients often present with bone pain and proximal muscle weakness, which is aggravated by physical activity. Levels of 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D are often normal, and its measurement (Answer A) is therefore not helpful in making the diagnosis. Patients with osteomalacia may present with reduced bone density on radiographic studies, with thinning of the cortex, and possible cortical stress fractures, often called Looser zones or pseudofractures. Bone density scans (Answer C) may show generalized osteopenia; however, this finding is nonspecific and does not distinguish between osteomalacia and osteoporosis. An iliac crest biopsy (Answer E) is not needed either, as it would reveal osteomalacia as well, without identifying a cause that could be elucidated more rapidly, simply, and less expensively with standard laboratory studies. Obtaining a bone scan (Answer D) to further evaluate her bone pain might be indicated in certain settings, such as in metastatic cancer or Paget disease. In the setting of high bone turnover, as would be the case in this vignette, bone scan will reveal a diffusely high uptake throughout the skeleton, including the tibia. Thus, it would not shed light on the correct diagnosis.

A 69-year-old man comes to you for evaluation of osteoporosis. He had a hip fracture 6 months ago and has since been treated with risedronate, 35 mg orally once weekly. His initial DXA scan shortly after his fracture, revealed the following T scores: lumbar spine, -1.9, femoral neck, -2.8, and total hip -2.6. He reports epigastric discomfort when he takes the risedronate, despite taking it on an empty stomach with a full glass of water and waiting at least 30 minutes before eating food or taking other medications. A trial of a proton-pump inhibitor has not improved his symptoms.

Laboratory test results:

Serum creatinine = 1.9 mg/dL (168.0 μmol/L)

Estimated glomerular filtration rate = 30 mL/min

25-Hydroxyvitamin D = 38 ng/mL (94.8 nmol/L)

Serum calcium = 10.0 mg/dL (2.5 mmol/L)

Which of the following should be your next step?

A. Continue risedronate

B. Discontinue risedronate and start calcitonin

C. Discontinue risedronate and start monthly oral ibandronate

D. Discontinue risedronate and start intravenous zoledronic acid

E. Discontinue risedronate and start denosumab

### Answer 111: E) Discontinue risedronate and start denosumab

## 111 ANSWER: E) Discontinue risedronate and start denosumab

Bisphosphonates are poorly absorbed orally, with less than 1% absorbed in the gastrointestinal tract. In addition, gastrointestinal adverse effects, including gastritis and esophagitis are relatively common. Often, these symptoms resolve within a few weeks or within a few doses. However, an alternative medication should be prescribed when gastrointestinal symptoms persist. Therefore, continuing the current medication (Answer A) is incorrect. Prescribing another oral bisphosphonate (Answer C) with a similar adverse effect profile when the patient has developed gastrointestinal adverse effects is not a tenable long-term solution. Moreover, ibandronate does not offer fracture protection at the hip. In this patient, intravenous zoledronic acid (Answer D) is desirable because in a trial of zoledronic acid for secondary hip fracture prevention, a mortality benefit was documented and the intravenous administration of the agent solves the issue of gastrointestinal intolerance; however, this patient's reduced renal function is a contraindication to use of zoledronic acid.

Given his history of hip fracture and low bone density in the osteoporotic range, he should receive treatment. Alternative therapies include calcitonin, teriparatide, or denosumab. In this patient, replacing risedronate with calcitonin (Answer B), which does not offer fracture protection at the hip, is not a good choice. Teriparatide (not an option offered in this vignette) is indicated to increase bone mass in men with primary or hypogonadal osteoporosis at high risk for fracture. Thus, it would be an option for this man. Denosumab (Answer E) given by injection once every 6 months, reduces the risk of fracture at both the hip and spine, as demonstrated in the FREEDOM trial. Because it is given by subcutaneous injection, it avoids the gastrointestinal adverse effects common to bisphosphonates and it can be used in the setting of chronic kidney disease.

112 Answer: Sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors constitute a newer category of diabetes therapy. Their use is currently indicated either in conjunction with lifestyle modification as monotherapy or with other medications. SGLT2 inhibitors lower blood glucose by decreasing SGLT2 activity in the proximal renal tubule. They lower the renal threshold for glucose and thereby increase glucose excretion in the urine and lower the plasma glucose concentration. This may cause polyuria and glucosuria. Canagliflozin should be taken before the initial meal of the day and can be started at a dosage of 100 mg daily and then increased to a maximum dosage of 300 mg daily, depending on normal renal function.

300 mg daily, up to 300 mg taking SGLT2 inhibitors can develop urinary tract infections related to glycosuria (approximately 5% incidence). This may be associated with symptoms of dysuria and polyuria. Urine analysis, as ordered in this vignette, helps to diagnose this adverse reaction, which should be treated with antibiotic therapy once confirmed. This patient's urine analysis documented that she does have a urinary tract infection. Because of SGLT2 inhibitors' mechanism of action, treated patients are also at an increased risk of developing vulvovaginal candidiasis, vulvovaginal mycotic infection, vulvovaginitis, and vulvitis (approximately 10% incidence). Men are at an increased risk of developing balanitis or balanopostitis and genital fungal infection. Patients who have a history of such infections or uncircumcised men are at an increased risk of developing these complications. Women usually report increased vaginal itching or discharge. Similar symptoms can be present in men. These symptoms are attributed to the medication and are easily treated with antifungal therapy such as fluconazole.

Occurrence of a single urinary tract infection or instance of genital mycosis does not necessitate discontinuing or reducing the dosage of SGLT2 inhibitor therapy (thus, Answers B and C are incorrect). Most episodes respond rapidly and do not recur. Thus, recommending no additional therapy now (Answer E) is appropriate.

Pelvic inflammatory disease, such as endometritis, is usually associated with lower abdominal pain that is often bilateral and exacerbated by sexual intercourse. Vaginal discharge, abnormal uterine bleeding, and systemic symptoms such as fever with chills may also occur. Lack of these symptoms makes this a very unlikely diagnosis. Endometritis is not an adverse reaction of SGLT2 inhibitors and the findings on urinalysis suggest a urinary tract infection (thus, Answer D is incorrect).

Intrauterine devices have relatively few adverse effects. In the past, intrauterine devices were linked with pelvic inflammatory disease, most likely because of the construction of braided and multifilament tails. These designs may have allowed bacteria to ascend the upper genital tract and produce infection. Currently available IUDs have monofilament tail strings and pelvic inflammatory disease is no longer a significant risk (thus, Answer A is incorrect).

## 117 ANSWER: D) Prednisone The critical element in this

The critical element in this vignette is that the patient does not have elevated PTH levels. His hypercalcemia is secondary to extrarenal conversion to 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D by activated macrophages, rather than primary hyperparathyroidism in which parathyroid autonomy leads to hypercalcemia and elevated PTH. Ectopic activation of vitamin D causes absorptive hypercalcemia and subsequently a suppressed PTH level. Phosphate absorption, like calcium absorption, tends to be increased (and because of hypoparathyroidism, urinary phosphate excretion is reduced), such that the serum calcium-phosphate product is elevated and ectopic calcification and nephrocalcinosis are relatively common.

Pneumocystis jirovecii (formerly Pneumocystis carinii) is an opportunistic infection associated with HIV infection and organ transplant. Hypercalcemia associated with P. jirovecii pneumonia can be severe, and it is due to the extrarenal conversion of 25-hydroxyvitamin D to 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D. The list of granulomatous disorders associated with HIV infection is extensive and includes tuberculosis, mycobacterium avium, histoplasmosis, and P. jirovecii. This patient has multiple manifestations of P. jirovecii pneumonia including fever, dyspnea, and fatigue. In addition, he has tachypnea, low oxygen saturation, and a chest radiograph demonstrating the presence of pneumonia. His CD₄ count is low, and he is not taking antiretroviral agents or P. jirovecii prophylaxis, thus putting him at increased risk for this opportunistic infection. In addition, he has symptomatic hypercalcemia (symptoms include fatigue, anorexia, and constipation).

Appropriate treatment of hypercalcemia in a patient with granulomatous disease and ectopic activation of vitamin D can generally be accomplished by aggressive fluid hydration and administration of glucocorticoids, such as prednisone (thus, Answer D is correct). The use of glucocorticoids is safe and effective in this setting. In addition, once a specific etiology for the granulomatous disease has been confirmed, P. jirovecii in this case, treatment directed at that pathogen (eg, intravenous trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole) should be initiated.

Because the basis for the hypercalcemia is excess absorption of calcium from the gut, which in turn lowers PTH, bone turnover in these individuals is often very low. Subcutaneous calcitonin works to decrease serum calcium levels by inhibiting osteoclastic bone resorption, and to a much lesser extent by increasing renal calcium excretion. Thus, the mechanism of action limits the benefit of calcitonin (Answer E).

Zoledronic acid (Answer B) effectively lowers serum calcium when excessive bone resorption is the cause of hypercalcemia. Similarly, denosumab (Answer C) lowers serum calcium in high bone turnover states, but would not reduce the conversion of 25-hydroxyvitamin D to 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D. Neither agent would therefore be useful in this setting.

Cinacalcet (Answer A) can lower calcium levels in patients with hyperparathyroidism because it sensitizes the parathyroid cells to extracellular calcium. In this patient who has a suppressed PTH level, it would be of no benefit.
