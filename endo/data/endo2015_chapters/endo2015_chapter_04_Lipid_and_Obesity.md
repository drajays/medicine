### Answer 25: B) Increase his levothyroxine dosage This

## 25 ANSWER: B) Increase his levothyroxine dosage This

25 A This patient is concerned about continued weight gain. One potential explanation would be excessive glucocorticoid replacement, which can lead to multiple adverse effects including lower bone mineral density, lower lean muscle mass, and weight gain. Adequacy of supplementation is often determined by subjective clinical parameters that are nonspecific. In patients with cortisol deficiency who take what is deemed to be appropriate replacement therapy, scores in validated self-assessment surveys of wellness (SF36) are consistently lower than scores of age- and sex-matched control participants. The scores are lowest in patients with a central cause for adrenal insufficiency. However, in view of this patient's height and weight, his current steroid dosage is most appropriate and further reductions are not advised.

Testosterone deficiency is associated with changes in body composition such as increases in percentage of body fat and decreases in lean muscle mass. Replacement of gonadal steroids can improve lean muscle mass. However, this intervention alone is not expected to contribute to weight loss (thus, Answer E is incorrect). GH deficiency also contributes to unfavorable changes in body composition (decreased lean muscle mass). However, the decision to initiate GH supplementation is rarely related to weight management (thus, Answer C is incorrect).

A basal metabolic rate measurement via indirect calorimetry determines basal caloric requirements and can guide dietary therapy when patients attempt to lose weight. Because thyroid hormone is a determinant of energy expenditure, measurement of the basal metabolic rate can also provide information regarding the adequacy of thyroid hormone supplementation. This patient's resting energy expenditure was accurately measured. He achieved a steady state at 1844 kcal, representing 69% of his predicted values on the basis of his age, height, weight. Values less than 20% of predicted are consistent with a hypometabolic state. His levothyroxine dosage was therefore increased (Answer B is correct) to 150 mcg daily and subsequently to 175 mcg daily. In addition, regular physical activity, he was advised to limit his caloric intake to 1800 to 2200 kcal daily. Over the subsequent 6 months, he was able to lose 26.5 lb (12 kg). He felt well and motivated to continue his efforts. Another measurement of resting energy expenditure was planned in 6 to 12 months.

Proponents of combined $T_{4}$ and $T_{3}$ supplementation comment on improved patient well-being, yet there are data to support a weight benefit with this combination (thus. Answer D is incorrect).

DHEA is a steroid intermediate in the pathway of adrenal and gonadal steroid biosynthesis. DHEA supplements have been purported to improve a wide array of symptoms including fatigue, muscle strength, and reproductive function. However, it would not facilitate weight loss and would be more likely to be weight-neutral or cause weight gain (thus, Answer A is incorrect).

## Question 41

A 63-year-old man has recently moved to your city and presents to establish care. He has a history of hypertension and had a myocardial infarction 2 years ago. He has no angina or shortness of breath. He is moderately active and walks daily. He does not smoke cigarettes. Current medications include aspirin, metoprolol, and lisinopril. You note that he is not on a statin, but he reports that he previously tried simvastatin, atorvastatin, rosuvastatin, and ezetimibe + simvastatin, but had intolerable myalgias while taking each of these.

On physical examination, pulse rate is 84 beats/min and blood pressure is 128/84 mm Hg. Height is 66 in (167.6 cm), and weight is 220 lb (100 kg) (BMI = 35.5 kg/m $ ^{2} $). Physical examination findings are normal.

Laboratory test results (fasting):

Glucose = 115 mg/dL (6.38 mmol/L)

Hemoglobin  $ A_{lc}=6.3\% $ (0.063)

HDL cholesterol = 28 mg/dL (0.73 mmol/L)

Total cholesterol = 138 mg/dL (3.57 mmol/L)

LDL cholesterol = 75 mg/dL (1.94 mmol/L)

Triglycerides = 280 mg/dL (3.16 mmol/L)

In addition to recommending a calorie-restricted diet, which of the following should you prescribe?

A. Pravastatin

B. Fenofibrate

C. Fish oil

D. Ezetimibe

E. Niacin

### Answer 41: B) Fenofibrate

## 41 ANSWER: B) Fenofibrate

Despite his low LDL-cholesterol level, this man has already experienced a myocardial infarction and is at very high risk for further cardiovascular disease events. He does not smoke cigarettes, his blood pressure is controlled, and he is taking appropriate cardiovascular medications (aspirin,  $ \beta $-adrenergic blocker, ACE inhibitor). Thus, the only other modifiable cardiovascular risk factor is his dyslipidemia. Most guidelines recommend statins in patients with established cardiovascular disease to further reduce risk; the Adult Treatment Panel III (ATP

III) guidelines recommend treatment to achieve an LDL-cholesterol concentration less than 100 mg/dL (<2.59 mmol/L) with an optional goal of less than 70 mg/dL (<1.81 mmol/L). The newer American Heart Association American College of Cardiology guidelines have moved away from target-driven therapy, and instead recom割high-intensity statin therapy to further reduce risk in patients at high risk such as this one, regardless of LDL-cholesterol level. This recommendation is based in part on studies such as Treat to New Targets (TNT), which compared high-dosage vs low-dosage atorvastatin and found further reductions in events in participants with low LDL-cholesterol levels. However, this patient has had intolerable myalgias with at least 3 statins. Although he not tried pravastatin (Answer A), the fact that he had myalgias with atorvastatin, simvastatin, and rosuvastatin, which differ in their relative hydrophilicity, hepatic metabolism, clearance, and elimination half-life, suggests the will most likely not tolerate pravastatin either. The prevalence of muscle symptoms in statin users is reported to be 5% to 10%; however, the placebo groups generally have a similar prevalence of muscle symptoms, which reflects the high population prevalence of muscle symptoms. In general, if a patient has myalgias to one statin, trying another statin with different pharmacokinetic profiles is appropriate. However, if several have been tried they each cause the same reaction, a tolerable statin is unlikely to be found. A growing body of literature address intermittent statin dosing (such as alternate day) to increase statin tolerability; however, this has not yet been tested with respect to cardiovascular outcomes.

Although statins have the best evidence to support their reduction of cardiovascular disease events, other lip lowering agents can also improve outcomes. Ezetimibe (Answer D) is a cholesterol absorption inhibitor that low LDL cholesterol via a different mechanism than statins. Although this patient previously reported myalgias when taking a statin + ezetimibe combination pill, the symptoms were almost certainly due to the statin component. It would most likely tolerate ezetimibe as monotherapy. However, to date, ezetimibe has not been shown to decrease risk of cardiovascular events. Given that this patient's main lipid abnormalities are low HDL cholesterol and triglycerides, and his LDL-cholesterol level is already fairly low, further LDL lowering with an agent that has not yet been shown to improve outcomes is not the best choice.

Fish oil (Answer C) has been used for its triglyceride-lowering effects, but it is usually recommended for severe hypertriglyceridemia—when triglyceride concentrations are greater than 500 mg/dL (>5.65 mmol/L) and there is a risk of pancreatitis. Fish oil has not been shown to reduce cardiovascular events.

Fibrates (Answer B) have been studied in a variety of monotherapy trials for both primary and secondary prevention. Although fibrates have minimal effects on LDL cholesterol, they can lower triglycerides by up to 50% and raise HDL-cholesterol levels by 10% to 15%. In several studies, including the VA-HIT trial, fibrates reduced cardiovascular events. Similarly, niacin (Answer E), which can lower triglycerides and LDL cholesterol and raise HDL cholesterol, reduced recurrent cardiovascular events and mortality in the Coronary Drug Project. Thus, either fibrate or niacin therapy is a good alternative to statin therapy in statin-intolerant patients. There are no comparisons between the agents regarding cardiovascular risk reduction, and the decision of which agent to prescribe should thus be guided by potential adverse effects. Fibrates and niacin can cause myalgias and myopathy, and rhabdomyolysis has been reported; however, this risk tends to be greatest when either of these agents is used with a statin, which is not the plan in this vignette. Fibrates have been associated with elevations in transaminases or creatinine, and cholelithiasis may occur. Niacin is also associated with elevation in transaminases and is known to cause flushing, rash, diarrhea, nausea, and pruritus. Niacin can increase glucose levels and worsen insulin resistance, which may be a concern in this patient who has impaired fasting glucose as at high risk for diabetes. In general, fibrates are very well tolerated, with discontinuation rates of 5% or less trials. Conversely, niacin has been a study discontinuation rate of up to 16%, with more than 60% of patients reporting flushing. Thus, on the basis of this patient's lipid profile, the adverse effects of the agents, and the outcome of available, fenofibrate (Answer B) is the best choice.

### Answer 52: E) Continue the current statin at the same dosage

## 52 ANSWER: E) Continue the current statin at the same dosage

52 The issue in this vignette is the safety of statin use in patients who consume grapefruit (fruit or juice). The lay literature is ripe with danger warnings about the consumption of grapefruit while taking statins, and patients

are routinely counseled to avoid grapefruit or its juice when taking certain medications, including statins. Grapefruit and its juice contain a number of compounds that irreversibly inhibit CYP3A enzymes that are key in the metabolism of a number of drugs. Most statins undergo significant metabolism by the CYP3A system; thus, if the enzymes are inhibited, this leads to increased systemic plasma concentrations of statins, which has the potential to increase clinical and adverse effects. Because the CYP3A system is irreversibly inhibited, this can have effects on drug clearance for the several days after even a single dose. However, the impact of inhibiting CYP3A on statin metabolism is related to the clearance pathway and bioavailability of the specific agent. For example, simvastatin undergoes extensive first-line metabolism by CYP3A4, so its mean oral bioavailability is about 5%; inhibition of CYP3A4 could lead to significant increases—up to 10-fold—in its bioavailability. Conversely, atorvastatin has a higher mean oral bioavailability of 15% to 30%, so concentrations are only modestly increased by CYP3A inhibitors. Another consideration is how the grapefruit or juice does it take to have a significant impact on CYP3A? Several small pharmacokinetic studies suggest that it takes more than 500 mL of grapefruit juice on a daily basis to affect drug metabolism. Consumption of 600 to 750 mL of grapefruit juice each day has been shown to increase the plasma concentration of atorvastatin by 1.3-fold. However, it is quite rare to consume that much grapefruit or grapefruit juice on a regular basis. One study evaluated more commonly consumed amounts: one 300-mL (10-oz) glass of grapefruit juice per day for 90 days in long-term atorvastatin users. Participants were randomly assigned to continue their usual atorvastatin dosage or to decrease their baseline dosage by 50% when starting the grapefruit juice. Participants who continued their baseline dosage had an increase in atorvastatin plasma concentrations of up to 26% compared with concentrations at baseline with negligible changes in lipid levels and no adverse effects on liver function or serum creatine phosphokinase values. Participants randomly assigned to decrease their baseline dosage by 50% had a decrease in plasma atorvastatin concentrations of 12% to 25%, with an increase in LDL-cholesterol concentration (9%) and triglycerides (23%) compared with baseline concentrations. Again, no adverse effects on liver function or creatine phosphokinase levels were observed. Thus, on the basis of this study's findings, the Answer E is correct—no change in statin dosage is needed. The prescribing information raises concern with “excessive grapefruit juice consumption >1.2 liters/day.”

If this patient had any other risk factors such as use of other C. some calcium channel blockers, some macrolide antibiotics), were frail, or were taking multiple medications, then decreasing the statin dosage (Answer B) might be reasonable. Given this patient's high risk for a recurrent cardiovascular event, discontinuing the statin, even if starting an alternative agent such as a fibrate or ezetimibe, would not be appropriate (thus, Answers A, C, D are incorrect).

It is important to note that different statins are subject to different metabolism, and thus the response to grapefruit juice or other inhibitors of CYP3A4 is agent specific. Most studies evaluating the effect of grapefruit� statins are relatively short term (see Table).

### Answer 58: D) Calcium oxalate crystals

## 58 ANSWER: D) Calcium oxalate crystals

Enteric hyperoxaluria and calcium oxalate stone formation are well-recognized complications of malabsorptive bariatric operations such as the jejunoileal bypass. Until recently, the risk for hyperoxaluria and calcium oxalate stone formation with modern bariatric surgery, mainly Roux-en-Y gastric bypass, was unrecognized. Investigators have documented this association and report almost a doubling of the risk for the development of renal stone disease when compared with the risk of obese control participants. However, the overall risk for stone formation appears to be low even in patients with a history of renal stone disease and should not represent a contraindication to bariatric surgery. Ongoing investigation and surveillance is advised to identify patients.

The risk for renal stone disease is highest during the first 5 years after Roux-en-Y gastric bypass. The most common laboratory abnormalities reported include hyperoxaluria (>100 mg/24 h urine specimen), low urinary citrate (<500 mg/24 h urine specimen) (thus, Answer B is incorrect), and low urine volumes (<1.5 L) (thus, Answer E is incorrect). Patients with markedly low urine volumes (<1 L) can have substantial elevations in calcium oxalate supersaturations despite minimal abnormalities in urinary oxalate and citrate excretion. Although obesity is associated with higher risk for uric acid renal stones, the risks drops significantly after bariatric surgery and uric acid excretion is low (thus, Answer C is incorrect). Calcium excretion varies depending on dietary calcium intake and calcium supplementation, but it is rarely elevated (thus, Answer A is incorrect).

Increased calcium oxalate supersaturation was the most common abnormality reported in study examining a patient cohort after Roux-en-Y gastric bypass (thus, Answer D is correct). A proposed mechanism for the observed hyperoxaluria is increased intestinal absorption of oxalate with diets low in calcium and high in fat. As with any renal stone disease, low urine volumes increase the risk. For at-risk patients, recommendations include sufficient fluid intake to maintain a urine volume greater than 2 L daily, restriction of dietary fat, and adequate calcium intake either from dietary sources or supplementation.

## 110 ANSWER: E) Measurement of apolipoprotein B or LDL particle number

Although laboratory and clinical parameter-based assessments of cardiovascular risk have been validated in a wide range of populations, they are not perfect. Individuals who have low predicted risk might still experience a cardiovascular event; conversely, individuals who have high predicted risk may never have an event, even when risk-reducing therapies (such as lipid-lowering therapies) are not used. Thus, there is an ongoing interest in additional means to assess cardiovascular risk, especially in situations such as the one described in this vignette—this patient scores at “intermediate” risk using the Framingham risk calculator.

Apolipoprotein B (Answer E) is the main structural protein for atherogenic lipoproteins (VLDL, IDL, LDL), but it is not present in HDL. There is 1 apolipoprotein B molecule per lipoprotein particle, so apolipoprotein B indicates particle number, whereas the LDL cholesterol indicates the amount of cholesterol found in the LDL fraction. An LDL-cholesterol concentration of 100 mg/dL (2.59 mmol/L) could be due to many smaller particles (with less cholesterol per particle) or fewer larger particles (with proportionately more cholesterol per particle). Because the smaller, denser LDL particles are thought to be more atherogenic than larger, more buoyant particles.

there has been an emerging interest in measuring particle number, and several commercial labs provide this service. The INTERHEART study was a large international study of cardiovascular risk in which apolipoprotein B levels were found to be a significant predictor of myocardial infarction, even after adjusting for age and traditional lipid risk factors. However, other studies have refuted this finding, so it remains controversial. There is potential redundancy because measurement of apolipoprotein B or LDL particle number can be approximated by the use of the non-HDL-cholesterol concentration: if the non-HDL-cholesterol level is more than 30 mg/dL (0.78 mmol/L) above the LDL-cholesterol level, this indicates high particle number. Some guidelines have recommended measurement of apolipoprotein B or LDL particle number, particularly in patients at high risk, to enhance risk assessment and provide apolipoprotein B goals for therapy. Although routine measurement of apolipoprotein B or LDL particle number has not been uniformly adopted, there is some suggestion in the literature that determining these values may be appropriate in individuals at very high risk, in those with hypertriglyceridemia, or in those with genetic dyslipidemias. Although cost-effectiveness for mass screening has not been formally assessed for apolipoprotein B measurement, it is relatively low-cost assay and is well validated in several populations. In this patient with elevated triglycerides, it may more accurately assess risk than measurement of LDL cholesterol or non-HDL cholesterol, and it is the best choice of the options presented here. However, it is important to note that measurement of apolipoprotein B or LDL particle number is not presently part of most guidelines. In fact, recent studies suggest that up to 75% of cholesterol laboratory testing could be avoided in a cost-effective strategy of cardiovascular risk assessment via a multistage screening approach using non-laboratory risk factors such as age, smoking status, blood pressure, and BMI.

The coronary artery calcium (CAC) score (Answer A) is determined by a CT-based scan that detects calcium, which indicates atherosclerosis in asymptomatic individuals. It has been proposed as a tool to detect disease (and thus risk for cardiovascular events) in persons who may be incorrectly assessed as being at low risk by traditional risk factors such as the Framingham score. Meta-analyses of CAC studies have found correlations between CAC scores and cardiac outcomes, with a relative risk of a cardiac event of 4.4 if CAC is present vs absent. As the CAC score increases, so does the risk of cardiac events. Furthermore, the CAC score varies between individuals even if they have the same or similar Framingham risk scores. However, the CAC score itself is not perfect because it only detects calcified plaque, and many dangerous (at risk of rupturing) atherosclerotic plaque lesions are not yet calcified; thus, the CAC score can underestimate disease. Conversely, CAC scores can overestimate risk in calcified low-risk-for-rupturing lesions. Cost-effectiveness analyses of CAC scoring have not supported its use, and there are health concerns with the procedure itself because of radiation exposure. Therefore, CAC scoring is not routinely recommended, nor is it cost-effective. There is no evidence that knowledge of CAC score improves adherence to lifestyle measures.

HDL has been long regarded as the protective lipid particle, and epidemiologic studies demonstrate that a low HDL-cholesterol concentration is associated with increased cardiovascular events. Conversely, higher HDL-cholesterol levels are thought to be protective. However, recent studies attempting to raise HDL cholesterol using fibrates (ACCORD [Action to Control Cardiovascular Risk in Diabetes]) or niacin (AIM-HIGH [Atherosclerosis Intervention in Metabolic Syndrome with Low HDL/High Triglyceride and Impact on Global Health Outcomes]) in statin-treated patients have not shown any additional benefit in cardiovascular outcomes, thus casting doubt on the utility of HDL-cholesterol measurement. There is an emerging interest in assessment of HDL function (Answer B), which includes antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and reverse cholesterol transport activities. However, to date these assessments are available in research laboratories only, not in clinical practice.

Coronary CT angiography (CCTA) (Answer C) is a newer tool using CT imaging to assess the presence of coronary artery disease. A recent study evaluated outcomes in more than 24,000 patients without known cardiovascular disease and determined that the absence of coronary disease was associated with a favorable prognosis. The study also documented a correlation between the number of coronary vessels with obstructive coronary artery disease and mortality. Thus, CCTA is emerging as a new tool to diagnose coronary artery disease and stratify risk in persons with detected coronary artery disease. Findings from additional studies comparing CCTA with invasive coronary angiography in patients with coronary symptoms have suggested that CCTA may have better utility. At this time, there are no cost-effectiveness data to support CCTA screening in primary prevention.
