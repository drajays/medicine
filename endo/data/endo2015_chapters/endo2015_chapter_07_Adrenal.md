14 ANSWER: A) Desogestrel (0.15 mg) and ethinyl estradiol (0.03 mg) combined oral contraceptive pill. The combination of oligomenorrhea, elevated BMI, and clinical and biochemical evidence of hyperandrogenism make polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) a possible diagnosis. However, the high concentration of 17α-hydroxyprogesterone suggests that nonclassic (also known as late-onset) congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH) is, in fact, the etiology of this patient's symptoms. This could be confirmed by performing an ACTH-stimulation test, which would demonstrate a further rise in 17α-hydroxyprogesterone concentrations.

Late-onset CAH usually presents in late childhood, adolescence, or early adulthood and can be clinically indistinguishable from PCOS. Therefore, late-onset CAH should be excluded in all women who present with appt PCOS by measuring plasma 17α-hydroxyprogesterone (normal in PCOS and elevated in nonclassic CAH because reduced 21α-hydroxylase activity). However, one must be careful when interpreting 17α-hydroxyprogesterone level in premenopausal women. During the luteal phase, levels can approach 300 ng/dL (9.1 nmol/L), but the concentral should never exceed 1000 ng/dL (30.3 nmol/L) as was observed in this vignette.

Nonclassic CAH is inherited in an autosomal recessive manner and its prevalence varies according to ethm. Overall, the prevalence is estimated to be 1 in 1000 in white populations, but as high as 1 in 27 in women of Eastern European Jewish descent and 1 in 40 among persons of Hispanic ancestry.

In nonclassic CAH, mutations in the CYP21A2 gene lead to reduced, but not absent, 21α-hydroxylase activity. Disease-causing mutations are characterized as severe or mild, depending on the amount of residual enzyme activity. Persons with late-onset CAH typically have either 2 mild mutations or 1 mild mutation and 1 severe mutation, resulting in 20% to 60% of normal enzymatic activity. Therefore, adrenal cortisol production is maintained at the expense of increased ACTH drive to the adrenal, which leads to androgen excess and increased levels of precursor steroid such as 17α-hydroxyprogesterone. In contrast, individuals with classic CAH present birth with ambiguous genitalia (in girls) and adrenal insufficiency due to absent 21α-hydroxylase activity.

The young woman in this vignette has menstrual disturbance and other clinical features of androgen excess (facial acne and hirsutism). Given that she does not currently desire pregnancy, treatment should be aimed at addressing these symptoms. It is clear in the vignette that menstrual disturbance is the predominant problem, so the combined oral contraceptive pill (Answer A) is the most appropriate treatment. The oral contraceptive pill effectively suppresses ovarian androgens and thus may also address her acne and hirsutism.

Because women with nonclassic CAH have normal cortisol production, routine corticosteroid treatment to ensure adequate glucocorticoid levels is not required. However, exogenous glucocorticoid inhibition of ACTH also reduces adrenal androgen production (and 17α-hydroxyprogesterone) and would thereby alleviate her symptoms of hyperandrogenism and restore a normal menstrual cycle. However, glucocorticoid therapy is less appropriate than oral contraceptives for this patient. She does not currently desire fertility and glucocorticoid therapy would make her potentially fertile, thereby necessitating birth control if she were to become sexually active. A number of glucocorticoid regimens have been used for patients with CAH who desire pregnancy, including dexamethasone at bedtime (Answer D). However, the dosage used in these circumstances is usually 250 mcg. Thus, 2 mg nightly represents gross overreplacement and would result in iatrogenic Cushing syndrome.

Metformin (Answer C) can be used in the management of PCOS, but it is not recommended for the management of oligomenorrhea or hirsutism in CAH because the hyperandrogenism is not related to insulin resistance. Treatment should be directed toward reduction of androgen production, not blockade of androgen action as a first approach to treatment. Thus, spironolactone (Answer E) is incorrect. A long-acting GnRH analogue (Answer B) will have no effect on adrenal androgen production.

## 37 ANSWER: C) Presence of TPO antibodies

37 Subclinical hypothyroidism is diagnosed when a patient has an elevated serum TSH value accompanied by normal thyroid hormone levels. The natural history of subclinical hypothyroidism is particularly affected by 2 factors: the magnitude of the serum TSH elevation and the presence or absence of TPO antibodies.

Regarding TSH elevation, the study showed that 52% of patients documented to have baseline serum TSH values that were mildly elevated (5.0-9.9 mIU/L) had normal TSH values at the end of a 3-year period. The percentage of baseline TSH values in the ranges of 10.0 to 14.9 mIU/L and 15.0 to 19.9 mIU/L that normalized after 3 years in the same study were 13% and 5%, respectively. These observations have been confirmed in other studies. For example, in a study of patients older than 65 years, resolution of subclinical hypothyroidism occurred at the 2-year follow-up in 46% of those with TSH values between 4.5 and 6.9 mIU/L, in 10% of those with values between 7.0 and 9.9 mIU/L, and in 7% of those with values of 10.0 mIU/L or greater.

Nevertheless, there is an increased risk of developing overt hypothyroidism even with successively increasing baseline TSH values that fall within the normal range. This risk is affected by TPO antibody status. A study examined the risk of developing overt hypothyroidism during a longer follow-up period of 13 years according to initial TSH groupings of less than 2.5 mIU/L, 2.5 to 4.0 mIU/L, and greater than 4.0 mIU/L in patients with positive thyroid antibodies. Compared with an odds ratio of 1.4 in the group with TSH values less than 2.5 mIU/L, the odds ratio for hypothyroidism was 10 in the group with TSH values between 2.5 and 4.0 mIU/L and 108 for the group with TSH values greater than 4.0 mIU/L. The odds ratios were lower (1, 1.8, and 50, respectively) for the same TSH groups in patients without thyroid antibodies. Thus, some patients with mild TSH elevation, such as this patient, progress to overt hypothyroidism over prolonged periods and monitoring of thyroid function is indicated. A prospective study showed that the calculated risk ratios of overt hypothyroidism in the 3 TSH categories of less than 6.0 mIU/L, 6.0 to 12.0 mIU/L, and greater than 12.0 mIU/L were 1.2, 6.4, and 11.5, respectively, over 10 years of follow-up. These risk ratios increased to 5.3, 10.5, and 15.6 in patients with markedly elevated TPO antibody titers. Similarly, in one of the aforementioned studies, progression to overt hypothyroidism occurred with greater frequency in those with positive thyroid autoantibodies. Conversely, another study demonstrated that resolution of subclinical hypothyroidism is less likely in patients older than 65 years who have positive TPO antibodies than in those with negative antibodies (15% vs 48% of patients) (thus, Answer C is correct). The results of such studies have led to the recommendation to consider measurement of TPO antibodies as part of the evaluation of patients who have subclinical hypothyroidism.

However, in all these studies, patient age did not appear to predict progression to overt hypothyroidism (thus, Answer B is incorrect). Patient sex does not affect the likelihood of progression in some studies, although other studies do find a greater risk of progression in women. The reason for these discrepant findings with respect to sex may relate to the association between female sex and TPO antibodies, such that they are not independent factors. In the studies that have examined BMI, this factor did not affect the risk of developing overt hypothyroidism (thus, Answer E is incorrect). In some studies, the presence of a goiter increases the risk of future hypothyroidism. In one study of populations with different iodine intakes, a high-iodine intake is a further risk factor for development of overt hypothyroidism in patients with TPO antibodies (thus, Answer D is incorrect). Smoking has either been found to have no influence on progression to overt hypothyroidism or has been associated with less likelihood of having TPO antibodies (thus, Answer A is incorrect).

## 40 ANSWER: D) Increase dexamethasone to 0.5 mg at bedtime

In nonclassic congenital adrenal hyperplasia, the adrenal zona fasciculata's production of cortisol is partially reduced such that the hypothalamic-pituitary drive to the adrenal gland is increased to restore normal cortisol levels.

In particular, with disturbances in 21-hydroxylase activity, ACTH secretion rises above normal and 17-hydroxyprogesterone and DHEA-S are increased because they are proximate to the enzymatic defect. DHEA-S is a weak androgen, so elevated DHEA-S levels can inhibit hypothalamic GnRH, pituitary LH, and testicular testosterone secretion.

Nonclassic congenital adrenal hyperplasia is not life-threatening because of compensated production of glucocorticoids. It presents in childhood or early adulthood. In females, elevated adrenal androgen production can manifest with acne, hirsutism, and irregular menses. In males, adrenal androgen production can increase before the time of normal puberty and lead to a premature growth spurt and virilization. Hypertension occurs, albeit infrequently, if intermediate molecules with mineralocorticoid activity are increased.

The central question related to this vignette focuses on glucocorticoid replacement when symptoms from the partial enzymatic defect arise. For this patient, adrenal androgen production initially resulted in premature puberty and later in reduced gonadal function and erectile dysfunction. To address his concerns, longer-acting glucocorticoid medications should be administered at bedtime with a goal of reducing early-morning ACTH secretion and subsequent adrenal hormone synthesis. Generally, 1 mg of dexamethasone taken at bedtime will suppress most, if not all, ACTH secretion in normal individuals. Hence, dexamethasone dosages below 1 mg daily are often sufficient to prevent adrenal androgen hypersecretion in patients with nonclassic congenital adrenal hyperplasia. However, metabolism of dexamethasone varies among individuals, and some patients require more, or less, dexamethasone to experience a satisfactory response without signs and symptoms of glucocorticoid excess.

This patient was clearly undertreated at a dexamethasone dosage of 0.25 mg at bedtime. Thus, an increase to 0.5 mg at bedtime would be a reasonable next step (thus, Answer D is correct). Because of dexamethasone's long duration of action, splitting it into 2 doses is not needed and taking the dexamethasone in the morning would exert a suppressive action on ACTH at a time when it would not be elevated (ie, the afternoon and evening) (thus, Answer E is incorrect).

Generally, the ratio of glucocorticoid action for dexamethasone to hydrocortisone is ~25:1. A dose of 12.5 mg of hydrocortisone is roughly equivalent to 0.5 mg of dexamethasone. However, one would not use hydrocortisone at bedtime to suppress ACTH secretion because of its shorter duration of action (thus, Answer A is incorrect). Similar to splitting the dexamethasone dose, using twice-daily hydrocortisone is also unlikely to be helpful (thus, Answer B is incorrect).

Adding testosterone (Answer C) would increase circulating testosterone levels and might improve the erectile dysfunction. However, adding an additional medication should not be needed if the dexamethasone dosage is properly adjusted.

### Answer 62: B) Discontinue the oral contraceptive. The assessment of elevated steroid concentrations must always account for the presence of factors (including medications) that may increase the levels of steroid-binding proteins, conditions that alter metabolic clearance of steroids, or medications that may directly affect steroid-receptor binding. Primary aldosteronism is very common among persons with drug-resistant hypertension and this is one of the criteria that should prompt screening for this condition. Once primary aldosteronism has been confirmed, further tests are needed to establish whether aldosterone excess is due to bilateral adrenal hyperplasia (idiopathic primary aldosteronism) or an aldosterone-producing adenoma. This distinction is very important because bilateral adrenal hyperplasia should be treated medically with mineralocorticoid antagonists, whereas unilateral aldosterone-producing adenomas can be cured (or at least improved) with unilateral adrenalectomy.

The adrenal CT in this vignette demonstrates a small left adrenal adenoma, which may be an aldosterone-producing adenoma potentially amenable to surgical treatment. However, given the incidence of adrenal incidentaloma in persons older than 40 years, as well as the fact that CT is not sufficiently sensitive to detect adrenal micronodules that may be present in bilateral adrenal hyperplasia, it is recommended that patients older than 40 years undergo further testing with adrenal venous sampling (AVS) to confirm unilateral aldosterone excess before surgery is recommended. This can be done with or without cosyntropin stimulation, as there is conflicting evidence as to whether cosyntropin improves the diagnostic accuracy of AVS. However, it is important to know whether cosyntropin has been used to aid interpretation of AVS as detailed in the following text.

The key to answering the vignette's question is the accurate interpretation of the AVS results; some guidance is outlined:

## 1. Ensure both adrenal veins have been cannulated (Selectivity Index)

Measurement of the ratio of cortisol in the adrenal vein to cortisol in the inferior vena cava calculates the selectivity index. If adrenal vein cannulation is successful, cortisol measurements should be significantly higher here than in the inferior vena cava. A selectivity index cutoff greater than 2 (with cosyntropin) or 3 (without cosyntropin) suggests successful cannulation. Some centers also advocate the measurement of plasma epinephrine in the adrenal veins and peripheral circulation to confirm successful cannulation.

## 2. Determine whether there is unilateral aldosterone excess (Lateralization Index)

To do this, the ratio of aldosterone to cortisol must first be calculated within each adrenal vein. These so-called cortisol-corrected ratios of aldosterone are performed to overcome the potential dilution of aldosterone levels within the adrenal veins by blood flow from the accessory hepatic and inferior phrenic veins. Subsequently, the aldosterone to cortisol ratios between the affected and unaffected side should be calculated (the lateralization index). Current guidelines suggest that a lateralization index greater than 2 without cosyntropin and greater than 4 with cosyntropin is suggestive of lateralized aldosterone excess.

In this vignette, the selectivity index on the right side is 0.9, which suggests that the right adrenal vein was not cannulated. This is a common problem given the small size and awkward positioning of the right adrenal vein. Therefore, although it would appear that the left adrenal is producing excess aldosterone, this is impossible to confirm using these data.

The best course of action under these circumstances would be to recommend another AVS procedure (Answer D) or to simply commence medical therapy. However, the vignette emphasizes that the patient would prefer a surgical cure if possible. Thus, in this situation, repeated AVS is the correct step.

Aldosterone-producing adenomas and benign adrenal adenomas are not fluorodeoxyglucose-PET avid, so a PET-CT scan (Answer A) would not help in securing the correct diagnosis. There has, however, been some evidence that specialized PET-CT scanning using 11C-metomidate tracer may help identify aldosterone-producing adenomas; however, such scans are not currently routinely available and require further evaluation. Bilateral adrenalectomy (Answer C) is never an appropriate treatment of primary aldosteronism, even if there is evidence of bilateral aldosterone excess. Although some evidence in the vignette supports the possibility of a left aldosterone-producing adenoma, the AVS data have not confirmed lateralization of aldosterone excess to the left side. Therefore, to recommend left adrenalectomy (Answer B) on the basis of this information would be wrong, as right-sided aldosterone excess (and hence bilateral adrenal hyperplasia) has not been excluded. If this were bilateral adrenal hyperplasia, left adrenalectomy would not be curative and would expose the patient to an unnecessary and irreversible surgical procedure.

Rarely, aldosterone excess can be caused by an autosomal dominant disorder termed glucocorticoid remediable aldosteronism. It is due to the presence of a chimeric gene incorporating the promoter region of the CYP11B1 gene (11β-hydroxylase) and the remainder of the CYP11B2 gene (aldosterone synthase). Therefore, aldosterone excess develops under control of ACTH, resulting in hypertension. Glucocorticoid remediable aldosteronism is not likely in this vignette because it is far less common than nonfamilial primary aldosteronism and it is not usually associated with adrenal adenomas. Moreover, although dexamethasone suppression testing (to demonstrate ACTH sensitivity of aldosterone excess) (Answer E) was previously the main method of diagnosing this rare condition, current Endocrine Society guidelines recommend genetic testing as the criterion standard diagnostic tool for this condition.
