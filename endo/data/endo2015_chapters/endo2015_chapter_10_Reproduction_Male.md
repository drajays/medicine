### Answer 1: E) No change in current treatment

#### ANSWER: E) No change in current treatment

The use of a mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist such as spironolactone or eplerenone improves blood pressure and potassium homeostasis in patients with primary hyperaldosteronism. It is less well appreciated that there is a decline in glomerular filtration rate in the early phase after treatment initiation. This observation has been explained by aldosterone-induced hyperfiltration, which is abolished with either surgery or mineralocorticoid receptor blockade. The observed glomerular hyperfiltration in patients with primary hyperaldosteronism is associated with decreased intrarenal vascular resistance. The decline in glomerular filtration rate after treatment is significantly lower in patients with low pretreatment plasma aldosterone levels. Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists can also decrease serum sodium levels indicating the reversal of aldosterone-induced sodium-retinic effects and hypervolemia. Urinary albumin excretion decreases in patients who are treated with mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists.

Despite this initial decrease in glomerular filtration rate in patients treated for primary aldosteronism, the decline in renal function appears to be reversible and long-term renal function stabilizes. Therefore, no change in the patient's current treatment is indicated (Answer E). Substitution of eplerenone for spironolactone (Answer A) would not be expected to provide any additional benefit. In fact, on a milligram-for-milligram basis, higher dose of eplerenone are usually needed to adequately treat primary aldosteronism.

## 02 ANSWER: B) FNAB of the 1.2-cm nodule

The incidence of differentiated thyroid cancer has been increasing in recent years, at least in part due to increased diagnosis. Thyroid cancer is predicted to become the third most common malignancy diagnosed in women and to exceed the incidence of colorectal cancer by the year 2019. Despite the steadily rising incidence of thyroid cancer, the number of deaths caused by thyroid cancer has remained relatively stable. Although this patient's imaging was performed because of a palpable thyroid nodule, her malignant nodule was actually incidentally found.

This patient's 2.2-cm cystic nodule does not require FNAB (thus, Answers A and C are incorrect). Purely cystic nodules without a mural component do not require FNAB, even if they are larger than 1.5 or 2.0 cm. Cystic composition and spongiform appearance is each an excellent predictor of a benign nodule. Cystic nodules are typically only biopsied if there are other components, such that the nodule is mixed cystic and solid, with the solid component being the target of the biopsy. The risk of malignancy in mixed cystic and solid nodules decreases as the cystic proportion of the nodule increases. Cystic nodules may require aspiration to alleviate compressive symptoms or pain if they increase in size. In addition, such nodules may be managed by surgical excision if the fluid reaccumulates after repeated aspirations.

The smaller 1.2-cm nodule shown in the vignette's image was incidentally found on ultrasonography initially performed to evaluate the palpable cystic nodule. This incidental nodule is suspicious in many ways. It has microcalcifications, irregular borders, and hypoechogenicity. Thus, this nodule should be biopsied now (Answer B) despite the fact that it was incidentally found and is not much larger than 1.0 cm. Given the suspicious features of this nodule, simply obtaining follow-up ultrasonography in 6 months (Answer D) is incorrect. In a recent meta-analysis, nodules with taller than wide shape had the highest odds ratio of being malignant. Various other studies show that microcalcifications, irregular margins, and taller than wide structure have good specificity for malignancy (approximately 89%-92%). Hypoechogenicity of nodules has a reasonable sensitivity for malignancy. Extrathyroidal extension and abnormal cervical lymph nodes are particularly worrisome features. In the case of a patient with multiple thyroid nodules, the nodules should be prioritized for biopsy on the basis of presence or absence of the features described above and the size. The risk of malignancy is thought to be similar regardless of whether the patient has a solitary nodule or a multinodular gland.

A thyroid scan and uptake (scintigraphy) (Answer E) will determine whether a nodule is functioning or nonfunctioning, but is not the best test to determine whether a thyroid nodule is malignant. Although many thyroid cancers have decreased isotope uptake compared with the surrounding thyroid tissue (i.e., appear "cold"), other lesions, such as the larger cystic nodule present in this patient, may also appear "cold" with such imaging.

Diabetic ketoacidosis is a life-threatening complication of severe insulin deficiency that is commonly seen in patients with newly diagnosed type 1 diabetes. However, it can also occur in patients with type 1 diabetes who interrupt basal and bolus insulin for more than 24 hours, or in those on insulin pump therapy whose rapid-acting insulin analogue infusion is interrupted for more than 4 to 6 hours. Diabetic ketoacidosis may also complicate severe stressful illness in patients with type 1 or type 2 diabetes.

The standard of treatment of severe diabetic ketoacidosis involves aggressive fluid resuscitation; intravenous insulin; repletion of potassium; and close monitoring of hydration status, glucose, acidosis, and electrolytes. Following serum carbon dioxide or venous pH has mostly replaced following the arterial pH (Answer A) to assess adequacy of therapy. This avoids the pain and potential complications of serial arterial blood gas determination.

Venous pH is about 0.03 units lower than arterial pH, a nonclinically significant difference. Insulin therapy is the primary intervention to correct acidosis and it acts by inducing the metabolism of the ketoacids  $ \beta $-hydroxybutyrate and acetoacetate to bicarbonate. Insulin directly inhibits lipolysis and ketogenesis, promotes clearance of free fatty acids, and inhibits fatty acid oxidation to ketones in the liver. Administration of bicarbonate (Answer B) to patients with diabetic ketoacidosis who present with a pH as low as 6.9 has not been shown to speed correction of acidosis or reduce mortality. No randomized controlled studies of bicarbonate use have been performed in patients with a pH below 6.9. In those with marked hyperkalemia complicating ketoacidosis, bicarbonate can be used to accelerate the intracellular translocation of potassium from the intravascular space.

When treating ketoacidosis, intravenous insulin must be continued until the acidosis has cleared. To prevent hypoglycemia from occurring with continued intravenous insulin infusion, it is recommended that 5% dextrose be added to intravenous fluids when glucose levels fall below 200 mg/dL (<11.10 mmol/L) (thus, Answer C is correct). Normalization of acidosis, ketosis, and hyperglycemia is similar when intravenous regular insulin is compared with intravenous rapid-acting insulin (thus, Answer D is incorrect). In studies of patients with mild to moderate acidosis, equivalent rates of correction of acidosis, clearance of ketones, and glucose decline have been documented with intravenous regular insulin vs subcutaneous rapid-acting insulin administered every 1 to 2 hours. However, comparison of these 2 insulin regimens has not been well studied in patients with severe acidosis, in whom the rate of insulin administration can be more rapidly titrated with an intravenous insulin drip (thus, Answer E is incorrect).

Alterations in serum sodium are common in patients presenting with ketoacidosis. Hyperglycemia leads to the movement of intracellular water osmotically to the intravascular space, causing hyponatremia. Glucosuria leads to an osmotic diuresis of water in excess of sodium, increasing serum sodium. Hypernatremia is typically seen only with more severe hyperglycemia and dehydration. Marked hyperlipidemia can be seen in ketoacidosis, causing pseudohyponatremia as elevated lipids reduce the measured serum sodium concentration. Abnormalities of serum sodium in ketoacidosis correct after treatment with insulin and fluid resuscitation.

## 28 ANSWER: E) High-resolution and color-flow Doppler ultrasonography The use of amiodarone, an iodine-containing drug (27%)

The use of amiodarone, an iodine-containing drug (37% of its molecular weight), is complicated by the occurrence of thyroid dysfunction in 15% to 20% of cases, either in the form of thyrotoxicosis (amiodarone-

induced thyrotoxicosis [AIT] or hypothyroidism. AIT is more common in iodine-deficient areas and in men, at variance with spontaneous hyperthyroidism, whereas hypothyroidism is more common in iodine-sufficient areas. For a diagnosis of AIT, serum free  $ T_{4} $ and free  $ T_{3} $ concentrations must be increased and serum TSH must be suppressed. Two main forms of AIT exist: type 1 AIT, which is iodine-induced hyperthyroidism, and type 2 AIT, a destructive thyroiditis caused by toxic effects of amiodarone on thyroid cells. However, the 2 mechanisms may occur in the same patient, resulting in mixed or indefinite forms of AIT. Type 1 occurs in patients predisposed to thyroid autoimmunity or in those with preexisting thyroid nodules, whereas type 2 usually develops in patients without clinical, biochemical, and morphologic evidence of thyroid disease. AIT may develop early during amiodarone treatment, but it may also occur several months after drug withdrawal since amiodarone and its metabolites have a long half-life (up to 100 days) and are stored in tissues, most notably fat, from which they are released very slowly. Diagnostic testing to distinguish between the 2 forms is advisable because the treatment differs—type 1 AIT is treated with antithyroid drugs and type 2 AIT is treated with corticosteroids. Combined antithyroid drug and anti-inflammatory treatment is required in those who fail to respond to single modality treatment and in those in whom the type of the disease cannot be unequivocally determined. It is likely that subsequent therapy for the underlying thyroid disease is required in those with type 1 AIT, whereas subsequent hypothyroidism may ensue in those with type 2 AIT. The decision to stop amiodarone in the setting of thyrotoxicosis should be determined on an individual basis in consultation with the cardiologist, based on the presence or absence of effective antiarrhythmic therapy.

This patient most likely has type 2 AIT, the most common form, because he has no apparent predisposing factors to thyroid autoimmunity or any obvious thyroid abnormalities on clinical examination. Moreover, the very low uptake of radioactive iodine on  $ {}^{123} $I scanning is typically seen in patients with type 2 AIT, whereas low-normal, normal, or even increased uptake may be seen in type 1 AIT, despite the iodine load. High-resolution thyroid ultrasonography will be informative in terms of determining the presence of a diffuse or nodular goiter, even in the absence of clinically detectable thyroid enlargement or nodularity. Additionally, color-flow Doppler sonography (Answer E) is a very important diagnostic tool that usually indicates an absent hypervascular pattern in type 2 AIT in contrast to the increased vascularity and blood flow velocity in type 1 AIT. In general, determination of uptake of radioiodine and color-flow Doppler sonography are considered the most useful diagnostic techniques in the initial assessment.

The search for thyroid-directed autoantibodies is likely to be informative regarding the presence of underlying autoimmunity, and the determination of TSH-receptor antibodies is relevant only in patients with AIT whose underlying and preexisting thyroid disorder is Graves disease. Because the patient's history and the radioiodine uptake scan point towards type 2 AIT, Answer A would be less informative than color-flow Doppler sonography.

The measurement of urinary iodine excretion (Answer B) is of limited value, as this will be high because of the high iodine content of amiodarone. It would not aid in the identification of the underlying cause of thyrotoxicosis in this case.

Some centers advocate the use of technetium-99m 2-methoxyisobutyl isonitrile scintigraphy (Tc-99m-MIBI) (Answer C) as a useful diagnostic tool. In a study of 20 consecutive patients with AIT, MIBI diffuse retention, which is indicative of hyperfunctioning tissue, was present in all patients with type 1 AIT. No significant uptake, suggestive of a destructive process, was found in patients with type 2 AIT. In those with mixed or indefinite forms, either faint persistence of MIBI uptake or a quick washout of the tracer was observed. The real usefulness of this expensive and complicated procedure in the differential diagnosis of AIT must be confirmed in larger studies, especially with respect to the diagnosis of more complex and mixed forms of AIT.

Interleukin 6 and C-reactive protein are markers of inflammation and are usually elevated in type 2 AIT. They may also be elevated in patients with type 1 AIT and in patients with subacute thyroiditis. In view of their lack of specificity, their measurement is less useful in this setting (thus, Answer D is incorrect).

### Answer 115: D) Buprenorphine

## 115 ANSWER: D) Buprenorphine

115 Chronic nonmalignant pain lasting longer than 6 months affects 15% to 30% of the population. Most of these patients benefit from combined treatment with physical modalities (warm compresses, acupuncture) and nonopioid analgesics (mainly nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory agents). However, approximately 20% to 25% of these patients do not benefit from traditional measures and require treatment with opioid analgesics. The last decade has seen a significant increase in the prescription of opioid analgesics for the treatment of noncancer and cancer-related chronic pain. As a result, physicians are increasingly encountering patients experiencing adverse effects of opioid therapy, including endocrine adverse effects. Although perturbations in adrenal, thyroid, and somatotropic axes have been reported, opioid-induced testosterone deficiency is the most commonly encountered endocrine adverse effect. Studies have shown that men taking opioid analgesics have testosterone levels that are half as high as testosterone levels of their age-matched counterparts taking nonopioid analgesics. This suppressive effect of opioid analgesics on gonadal hormones is seen regardless of their route of administration. Low testosterone levels are observed in 86% of men receiving intrathecal opioids, 89% receiving oral opioids, and 80% receiving transdermal opioids. In some patients, testosterone levels even decline into the castrate range (<50 ng/dL [<1.74 nmol/L]). Affected patients generally present with decreased libido, erectile dysfunction, and fatigue. In addition to sexual dysfunction, these patients also have a high prevalence of osteopenia and osteoporosis. As expected, men with opioid addiction experience similar hormonal perturbations as do patients on prescription opioid analgesics.

Opioid analgesics suppress testosterone levels by inhibiting the synthesis and secretion of GnRH; thus, most patients present with a hormonal pattern consistent with secondary hypogonadism (low or inappropriately normal gonadotropins with low serum testosterone levels). Evidence suggests that this suppression of GnRH synthesis is mediated via the  $ \mu $-opioid receptor. Hence, opioid analgesics that are agonists at the  $ \mu $-opioid receptor suppress the gonadal axis. These include natural derivatives of opium such as morphine (Answer E), semisynthetic opioid analgesics such as oxycodone (Answer B) and fentanyl (Answer C), and synthetic opioid analgesics such as methadone (Answer A). Hence, all of the above-mentioned medications have the potential to suppress serum testosterone levels in this patient and are therefore not the best choices.

Buprenorphine (Answer D) does not suppress the gonadal axis because of its unique pharmacologic profile. It is a partial agonist at the  $ \mu $-opioid receptor and an antagonist at the  $ \kappa $-opioid receptor. These differing effects at the

p- and x-opioid receptors are posited to be the mechanism behind its sparing of the gonadal axis. Buprenorphine is a potent opioid analgesic that was originally approved in the treatment of opioid dependence; however, it is used in the treatment of both noncancer and cancer-related chronic pain. In a study of men with opioid dependence, serum testosterone levels were normal in patients treated with buprenorphine and were comparable to levels in age-matched control participants (Figure 1). Methadone-treated patients had significantly lower levels of serum testosterone. As a result, both libido and erectile function were normal in most men on buprenorphine compared with abnormal function in those on methadone (Figure 2). The patient in this vignette experienced a substantial improvement in his back pain with buprenorphine, while his testosterone levels remained within the normal range.
