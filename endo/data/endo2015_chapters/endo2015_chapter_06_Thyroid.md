Adrenal insufficiency is a rare disorder that can be caused by autoimmune adrenalitis, infectious adrenalitis, hemorrhagic disease, metastatic invasion, and drugs. Autoimmune causes account for approximately 70% to 90% of adrenal insufficiency cases, although worldwide infectious causes are noted at a higher incidence.

Adrenal insufficiency is also known as Addison disease, so named after Thomas Addison who first described this condition. In this vignette, symptoms of nausea, weight loss, and unexplained hypoglycemia, along with the finding of moderate hypotension, should alert the physician to consider Addison disease. In addition, female gender and an existing diagnosis of type 1 diabetes make Addison disease a very likely possibility in this patient (polyglandular autoimmune syndrome type 2). ACTH stimulation testing can help confirm the diagnosis. Hyperpigmentation (see image) is associated with chronic ACTH stimulation and may occur in later stages of the disease. Persons with undiagnosed adrenal insufficiency can present with an acute adrenal crisis if they experience increased stress in the form of severe infection, infarction, or surgery.

This is a rare autosomal recessive disorder due

to mutations in the autoimmune regulator gene (AIRE). ## 32 ANSWER: E) Congenital adrenal hypoplasia

The combination of primary adrenal insufficiency and pubertal delay make congenital adrenal hypoplasia (Answer E) the most likely diagnosis. This is a rare disorder that occurs in approximately 1 in 12,500 live births and can present in 4 different ways:

1. Sporadic form, associated with pituitary hypoplasia

2. Autosomal recessive form

3. X-linked form, associated with hypogonadotropic hypogonadism

4. X-linked form, associated with glycerol kinase deficiency, Duchenne muscular dystrophy, and psychomotor retardation

This vignette illustrates an example of an X-linked form of congenital adrenal hypoplasia. Although congenital adrenal hypoplasia often presents in the neonatal period, the X-linked form may not present until in later childhood, adolescence, and even early adulthood. X-linked congenital adrenal hypoplasia is caused by deletion or mutations in the NROB1 gene (also known as DAX1), which is located on the short arm of the X chromosome. This part of the X chromosome also includes the glycerol kinase and Duchenne muscular dystrophy loci that may be deleted, resulting in more severe clinical consequences (although not in this clinical vignette).

The protein encoded by NR0B1, DAX1, is a nuclear receptor protein that is fundamental to adrenal and gonadal development and is expressed in the pituitary, hypothalamus, adrenals, and gonads. It acts as a dominant negative regulator of transcription of other nuclear receptors such as SF1 and is an antagonist of the protein encoded by SRY, a nuclear protein responsible for male sexual development. The disorder presents with adrenal insufficiency during the neonatal period in sporadic and autosomal recessive forms or later in life in the X-linked forms. Hypogonadotropic hypogonadism develops at puberty and is due to a combination of gonadal, pituitary, Sertoli-cell defects that develop because of DAX1 deficiency.

In a retrospective review of 103 children with adrenal insufficiency, the following causes were reported: congenital adrenal hyperplasia, 73%; Addison disease, 13%; peroxisomal disorders, 5%; lysosomal acid lipase deficiency (Wolman disease), 3%; unknown cause, 6%; and adrenal hypoplasia, 1%. Thus, adrenal insufficiency childhood is most often due to classic congenital adrenal hyperplasia.

In this vignette, a diagnosis of classic congenital adrenal hyperplasia due to 21α-hydroxylase deficiency (Answer D) is incorrect because this always presents in the neonatal period rather than during adolescence. In addition, testosterone production should not be affected by 21α-hydroxylase deficiency. However, affected patients can develop primary hypogonadism due to development to testicular rest tissue. There is no evidence of this clinically in the vignette and the biochemistry suggests secondary gonadal failure. Classic congenital adrenal hyperplasia caused by 11β-hydroxylase deficiency (Answer C) is also incorrect because this would present in infancy with adrenal failure and mineralocorticoid hypertension. Testosterone production is unaffected.

Although autoimmune adrenal failure and gonadal failure (autoimmune polyglandular syndrome) (Answer A) can present in children, primary adrenal failure at a young age should make the endocrinologist suspicious of a more unusual underlying cause. In addition, primary gonadal failure would occur in autoimmune polyglandular syndrome and this would be associated with elevated gonadotropin levels, which this patient does not have.

Peroxisomal disorders (adrenoleukodystrophy [Answer B] and adrenomyeloneuropathy) are rare X-linked disorders of β-oxidation that result in accumulation of very long chain fatty acids in tissues. They can present with adrenal insufficiency alone or in combination with spastic paraparesis (in adrenomyeloneuropathy) or severe neurologic features including quadriparesis, blindness, and severe cognitive dysfunction (in adrenoleukodystrophy). Adrenoleukodystrophy is an incorrect diagnosis given the lack of cognitive or neurologic features that would typically be seen by age 14 years. Although adrenal insufficiency can rarely be the only clinical feature of adrenoleukodystrophy, the presence of secondary hypogonadism makes congenital adrenal hypoplasia a more likely diagnosis.

53 Thyroid nodules occur in 20% to 75% of adults and 5% to 15% of these are proven to be malignant. FNAB is the most reliable clinical tool for determining whether a nodule is benign or malignant. However, approximately 10% to 25% of FNABs of thyroid nodules yield an indeterminate result. The Bethesda System for reporting thyroid cytopathology is used to classify these lesions as atypia of undetermined significance or follicular lesions of undetermined significance (AUS/FLUS—Bethesda Category III) or follicular neoplasm/suspicious for follicular neoplasm (FN/SFN—Bethesda Category IV). The reported risk of malignancy in these indeterminate lesions varies and is 7% to 48% for AUS/FLUS lesions and 20% to 30% for FN/SFN lesions. The Bethesda classification also labels lesions suspicious for malignancy as category V and the reported malignancy risk in these lesions is 50% to 75%. Recent advances in thyroid cancer biology have led to the development of several tests to assist in determining whether indeterminate nodules are benign or malignant. Currently, molecular tests to refine the diagnosis of thyroid malignancy are meant to complement and not to replace clinical judgment, sonographic assessment, and visual cytopathology interpretation.

Two main tests are currently marketed to assess for malignancy in thyroid nodules: gene expression classifiers (Answer B) and mutation analysis panels (Answer C). Gene expression classifiers are based on gene expression profiles of surgically proven benign and malignant thyroid nodules. They evaluate for the presence of a benign expression profile consisting of 142 mRNAs. Validation studies have shown high negative predictive values (94% to 95%) and sensitivity (90%) among nodules with the cytologic interpretation of AUS/FLUS or FN/SFN. Gene expression classifiers are therefore considered a “rule out” test to identify nodules that are benign. However, because a substantial number of benign nodules do not have a gene expression profile classified as benign, the test can therefore not always exclude malignancy. Moreover, clinical validation has not been performed for nodules smaller than 1 cm, nor has this form of testing been evaluated in the pediatric population. The use of this test has been reported to obviate the need for surgical excision in approximately 50% of cases. The specificity (52%) and positive predictive value (47%) of gene expression classifiers in nodules with indeterminate cytology are poor and their use is therefore not recommended to confirm the presence of malignancy. Preliminary cost analysis indicates that the use of gene expression classifiers may be cost-effective, although studies performed thus far have been largely based on preliminary data. the most appropriate of the listed options. The false-negative rate is 5% to 6%, so patients with a negative result should still be followed up closely with repeated ultrasonography.

BRAF Val600Glu and KAS point mutations, as well as common rearrangements of RET/PTC and PAX8/PPARG, are common drivers behind many thyroid cancers. It is estimated that 1 of these molecular parameters is present in 60% of well-differentiated thyroid cancer. The components of the mutation analysis panel are available as standalone tests or together as panels that assess for the presence of these molecular alterations simultaneously. Mutation analysis has high specificity and very good positive predictive value. ## 113 ANSWER: A) Perform FNAB of the palpable thyroid nodule

113 AN Patients presenting with nodular goiter should have a complete history, physical examination, and serum TSH measurement. Although neck palpation is inexpensive, widely available, and simple to perform, it is notoriously imprecise and insensitive in the detection and estimation of size and number of thyroid nodules. All patients with a nodular thyroid should be evaluated with ultrasonography to confirm the presence of a mass, to assess whether the lesion is single or multiple, to determine whether it is of thyroidal origin, and to guide FNAB—the procedure of choice in the evaluation of thyroid nodules. Patients with multiple thyroid nodules have the same risk of malignancy as those with solitary thyroid nodules. Additional nodules are detected in up to 50% of patients presenting with a single palpable nodule when ultrasonography is performed. Selection of those nodules that require biopsy in patients with a multinodular goiter requires careful consideration. Certain ultrasonographic features are associated with increased risk of malignancy, including hypoechogenicity, the presence of microcalcifications, taller than wide shape, increased vascular flow, irregular borders, and absence of a halo. Nodules with any of these features should be aspirated preferentially. The combination of several of these sonographic features increases specificity.

The patient in this vignette has multiple benign-appearing nodules that are well defined, isoechoic, and have no suspicious ultrasonographic features. In this situation, the best recommendation is to aspirate the largest or dominant nodule(s) and to observe the rest with periodic ultrasonography (Answer A). Nodules that have been reported to have benign cytologic characteristics have a false-negative rate of up to 5%; thus, follow-up is recommended within 6 to 18 months of the initial investigation. Although there is no consensus on the definition of clinically significant growth of a thyroid nodule, guidelines from the American Thyroid Association recommend another biopsy when there has been at least a 50% increase in nodule volume within 6 to 18 months from the initial FNAB. If the nodule size is stable, another FNAB is not required and the interval before the next clinical evaluation may be extended to 3 to 5 years.

The American Thyroid Association guidelines provide nodule threshold sizes for FNAB. For mixed cystic-solid nodules such as the ones found in this patient, the recommendation is to aspirate those nodules larger than 2 cm. However, size is not a specific feature to predict risk of malignancy. Since 2 of the 3 mixed cystic-solid nodules in this patient are smaller than 2 cm and have no suspicious features on ultrasonography, performing FNAB on all 3 nodules (Answer B) is incorrect. Notably, the recent guidelines from the British Thyroid Association do not use nodule size as a criterion for FNAB in thyroid nodules. The American Thyroid Association guidelines are currently under revision.

Serum thyroglobulin can be elevated in most thyroid disease and its measurement is a nonsensitive and nonspecific test for thyroid cancer. Although the serum thyroglobulin concentration is useful to monitor patients for residual or recurrent disease after treatment for thyroid cancer, it is not recommended in the initial evaluation of thyroid nodules (thus, Answer C is incorrect). Thyroglobulin antibodies occur in approximately 25% of patients with thyroid cancer and in 10% of the general population.

When present, they may falsely lower serum thyroglobulin concentrations in immunometric assays. Thyroglobulin antibodies should be quantitatively assessed with every thyroglobulin measurement.

Although it is important to follow-up the aspirated and nonaspirated nodules with ultrasonography, it is advisable to aspirate the largest dominant nodule. Ultrasonographic follow-up without aspiration of the largest nodule (Answer D) is not the most appropriate course of action.

In patients with nodular goiter and suppressed serum TSH concentrations, thyroid scanning with either technetium 99m pertechnetate or  $ {}^{123} $I should be performed to assess the functional status of thyroid nodules and to determine the presence of autonomous thyroid nodules. Thyroid scintigraphy (Answer E) has low diagnostic accuracy in the prediction of malignancy in thyroid nodules and is not recommended routinely in patients with normal serum TSH concentrations.
