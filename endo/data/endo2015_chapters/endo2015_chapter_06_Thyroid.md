### Answer 32: E) Congenital adrenal hypoplasia

## 32 ANSWER: E) Congenital adrenal hypoplasia

The combination of primary adrenal insufficiency and pubertal delay make congenital adrenal hypoplasia (Answer E) the most likely diagnosis. This is a rare disorder that occurs in approximately 1 in 12,500 live births and can present in 4 different ways:

1. Sporadic form, associated with pituitary hypoplasia

2. Autosomal recessive form

3. X-linked form, associated with hypogonadotropic hypogonadism

4. X-linked form, associated with glycerol kinase deficiency, Duchenne muscular dystrophy, and psychomotor retardation

This vignette illustrates an example of an X-linked form of congenital adrenal hypoplasia. Although congenital adrenal hypoplasia often presents in the neonatal period, the X-linked form may not present until in later childhood, adolescence, and even early adulthood. X-linked congenital adrenal hypoplasia is caused by deletion or mutations in the NROB1 gene (also known as DAX1), which is located on the short arm of the X chromosome. This part of the X chromosome also includes the glycerol kinase and Duchenne muscular dystrophy loci that may be deleted, resulting in more severe clinical consequences (although not in this clinical vignette).

The protein encoded by NR0B1, DAX1, is a nuclear receptor protein that is fundamental to adrenal and gonadal development and is expressed in the pituitary, hypothalamus, adrenals, and gonads. It acts as a dominant negative regulator of transcription of other nuclear receptors such as SF1 and is an antagonist of the protein encoded by SRY, a nuclear protein responsible for male sexual development. The disorder presents with adrenal insufficiency during the neonatal period in sporadic and autosomal recessive forms or later in life in the X-linked forms. Hypogonadotropic hypogonadism develops at puberty and is due to a combination of gonadal, pituitary, Sertoli-cell defects that develop because of DAX1 deficiency.

In a retrospective review of 103 children with adrenal insufficiency, the following causes were reported: congenital adrenal hyperplasia, 73%; Addison disease, 13%; peroxisomal disorders, 5%; lysosomal acid lipase deficiency (Wolman disease), 3%; unknown cause, 6%; and adrenal hypoplasia, 1%. Thus, adrenal insufficiency childhood is most often due to classic congenital adrenal hyperplasia.

In this vignette, a diagnosis of classic congenital adrenal hyperplasia due to 21α-hydroxylase deficiency (Answer D) is incorrect because this always presents in the neonatal period rather than during adolescence. In addition, testosterone production should not be affected by 21α-hydroxylase deficiency. However, affected patients can develop primary hypogonadism due to development to testicular rest tissue. There is no evidence of this clinically in the vignette and the biochemistry suggests secondary gonadal failure. Classic congenital adrenal hyperplasia caused by 11β-hydroxylase deficiency (Answer C) is also incorrect because this would present in infancy with adrenal failure and mineralocorticoid hypertension. Testosterone production is unaffected.

Although autoimmune adrenal failure and gonadal failure (autoimmune polyglandular syndrome) (Answer A) can present in children, primary adrenal failure at a young age should make the endocrinologist suspicious of a more unusual underlying cause. In addition, primary gonadal failure would occur in autoimmune polyglandular syndrome and this would be associated with elevated gonadotropin levels, which this patient does not have.

Peroxisomal disorders (adrenoleukodystrophy [Answer B] and adrenomyeloneuropathy) are rare X-linked disorders of β-oxidation that result in accumulation of very long chain fatty acids in tissues. They can present with adrenal insufficiency alone or in combination with spastic paraparesis (in adrenomyeloneuropathy) or severe neurologic features including quadriparesis, blindness, and severe cognitive dysfunction (in adrenoleukodystrophy). Adrenoleukodystrophy is an incorrect diagnosis given the lack of cognitive or neurologic features that would typically be seen by age 14 years. Although adrenal insufficiency can rarely be the only clinical feature of adrenoleukodystrophy, the presence of secondary hypogonadism makes congenital adrenal hypoplasia a more likely diagnosis.

## 37 ANSWER: C) Presence of TPO antibodies

37 Subclinical hypothyroidism is diagnosed when a patient has an elevated serum TSH value accompanied by normal thyroid hormone levels. The natural history of subclinical hypothyroidism is particularly affected by 2 factors: the magnitude of the serum TSH elevation and the presence or absence of TPO antibodies.

Regarding TSH elevation, the study showed that 52% of patients documented to have baseline serum TSH values that were mildly elevated (5.0-9.9 mIU/L) had normal TSH values at the end of a 3-year period. The percentage of baseline TSH values in the ranges of 10.0 to 14.9 mIU/L and 15.0 to 19.9 mIU/L that normalized after 3 years in the same study were 13% and 5%, respectively. These observations have been confirmed in other studies. For example, in a study of patients older than 65 years, resolution of subclinical hypothyroidism occurred at the 2-year follow-up in 46% of those with TSH values between 4.5 and 6.9 mIU/L, in 10% of those with values between 7.0 and 9.9 mIU/L, and in 7% of those with values of 10.0 mIU/L or greater.

Nevertheless, there is an increased risk of developing overt hypothyroidism even with successively increasing baseline TSH values that fall within the normal range. This risk is affected by TPO antibody status. A study examined the risk of developing overt hypothyroidism during a longer follow-up period of 13 years according to initial TSH groupings of less than 2.5 mIU/L, 2.5 to 4.0 mIU/L, and greater than 4.0 mIU/L in patients with positive thyroid antibodies. Compared with an odds ratio of 1.4 in the group with TSH values less than 2.5 mIU/L, the odds ratio for hypothyroidism was 10 in the group with TSH values between 2.5 and 4.0 mIU/L and 108 for the group with TSH values greater than 4.0 mIU/L. The odds ratios were lower (1, 1.8, and 50, respectively) for the same TSH groups in patients without thyroid antibodies. Thus, some patients with mild TSH elevation, such as this patient, progress to overt hypothyroidism over prolonged periods and monitoring of thyroid function is indicated. A prospective study showed that the calculated risk ratios of overt hypothyroidism in the 3 TSH categories of less than 6.0 mIU/L, 6.0 to 12.0 mIU/L, and greater than 12.0 mIU/L were 1.2, 6.4, and 11.5, respectively, over 10 years of follow-up. These risk ratios increased to 5.3, 10.5, and 15.6 in patients with markedly elevated TPO antibody titers. Similarly, in one of the aforementioned studies, progression to overt hypothyroidism occurred with greater frequency in those with positive thyroid autoantibodies. Conversely, another study demonstrated that resolution of subclinical hypothyroidism is less likely in patients older than 65 years who have positive TPO antibodies than in those with negative antibodies (15% vs 48% of patients) (thus, Answer C is correct). The results of such studies have led to the recommendation to consider measurement of TPO antibodies as part of the evaluation of patients who have subclinical hypothyroidism.

However, in all these studies, patient age did not appear to predict progression to overt hypothyroidism (thus, Answer B is incorrect). Patient sex does not affect the likelihood of progression in some studies, although other studies do find a greater risk of progression in women. The reason for these discrepant findings with respect to sex may relate to the association between female sex and TPO antibodies, such that they are not independent factors. In the studies that have examined BMI, this factor did not affect the risk of developing overt hypothyroidism (thus, Answer E is incorrect). In some studies, the presence of a goiter increases the risk of future hypothyroidism. In one study of populations with different iodine intakes, a high-iodine intake is a further risk factor for development of overt hypothyroidism in patients with TPO antibodies (thus, Answer D is incorrect). Smoking has either been found to have no influence on progression to overt hypothyroidism or has been associated with less likelihood of having TPO antibodies (thus, Answer A is incorrect).

53 Thyroid nodules occur in 20% to 75% of adults and 5% to 15% of these are proven to be malignant. FNAB is the most reliable clinical tool for determining whether a nodule is benign or malignant. However, approximately 10% to 25% of FNABs of thyroid nodules yield an indeterminate result. The Bethesda System for reporting thyroid cytopathology is used to classify these lesions as atypia of undetermined significance or follicular lesions of undetermined significance (AUS/FLUS—Bethesda Category III) or follicular neoplasm/suspicious for follicular neoplasm (FN/SFN—Bethesda Category IV). The reported risk of malignancy in these indeterminate lesions varies and is 7% to 48% for AUS/FLUS lesions and 20% to 30% for FN/SFN lesions. The Bethesda classification also labels lesions suspicious for malignancy as category V and the reported malignancy risk in these lesions is 50% to 75%. Recent advances in thyroid cancer biology have led to the development of several tests to assist in determining whether indeterminate nodules are benign or malignant. Currently, molecular tests to refine the diagnosis of thyroid malignancy are meant to complement and not to replace clinical judgment, sonographic assessment, and visual cytopathology interpretation.

Two main tests are currently marketed to assess for malignancy in thyroid nodules: gene expression classifiers (Answer B) and mutation analysis panels (Answer C). Gene expression classifiers are based on gene expression profiles of surgically proven benign and malignant thyroid nodules. They evaluate for the presence of a benign expression profile consisting of 142 mRNAs. Validation studies have shown high negative predictive values (94% to 95%) and sensitivity (90%) among nodules with the cytologic interpretation of AUS/FLUS or FN/SFN. Gene expression classifiers are therefore considered a “rule out” test to identify nodules that are benign. However, because a substantial number of benign nodules do not have a gene expression profile classified as benign, the test can therefore not always exclude malignancy. Moreover, clinical validation has not been performed for nodules smaller than 1 cm, nor has this form of testing been evaluated in the pediatric population. The use of this test has been reported to obviate the need for surgical excision in approximately 50% of cases. The specificity (52%) and positive predictive value (47%) of gene expression classifiers in nodules with indeterminate cytology are poor and their use is therefore not recommended to confirm the presence of malignancy. Preliminary cost analysis indicates that the use of gene expression classifiers may be cost-effective, although studies performed thus far have been largely based on preliminary data. Because the patient in this vignette has a lesion classified as FLUS (which carries a relatively low risk of malignancy), proceeding with a gene expression classifier test (Answer B) would be

the most appropriate of the listed options. The false-negative rate is 5% to 6%, so patients with a negative result should still be followed up closely with repeated ultrasonography.

BRAF Val600Glu and KAS point mutations, as well as common rearrangements of RET/PTC and PAX8/PPARG, are common drivers behind many thyroid cancers. It is estimated that 1 of these molecular parameters is present in 60% of well-differentiated thyroid cancer. The components of the mutation analysis panel are available as standalone tests or together as panels that assess for the presence of these molecular alterations simultaneously. Mutation analysis has high specificity and very good positive predictive value. However, because approximately 40% of malignant nodules with indeterminate cytology do not harbor any of these genetic mutations, they can reliably exclude malignancy (thus, Answers C and E are incorrect). They are considered good “rule-in” tests to confirm the presence of malignancy and may be used in lesions that are suspicious for malignancy. There is presently no consensus for the use of mutation testing as a guide to surgical decision-making, and mutation testing does not have a role in determining the extent of lymph node dissection. However, because the presence of a mutation such as BRAF Val600Glu has a positive predictive value of nearly 100% for the presence of papillary thyroid cancer, this finding might well justify the consideration of total rather than hemithyroidectomy.

The TSH receptor assay uses quantitative reverse transcription–polymerase chain reaction to detect circulating thyroid cancer cells by assessing the serum concentration of thyrotropin receptor mRNA from a standard peripheral blood specimen. This assay may be useful as a marker of thyroid cancer both in the setting of initial diagnosis and disease recurrence as a rule-out test. adrenomyeloneuropathy usually presents in adulthood with a peak age of onset of 20 to 40 years. Gonadal and adrenal dysfunction also typical.

The interpretation of thyroid function test results in pregnancy can be challenging because serum TSH concentrations fall in early pregnancy as a physiologic response to the stimulating effects of hCG on the TSH receptor with peak hCG concentrations between 7 and 11 weeks' gestation. Most studies also report a substantial decrease in serum free  $ T_{4} $ concentrations with the progression of gestation. Serum free  $ T_{4} $ measurements in pregnant women are complicated by increased thyroxine-binding globulin and decreased albumin concentrations, which result in marked interassay and interindividual variability when immune-mediated assays are used. Hence total  $ T_{4} $ measurement by extraction/liquid chromatography/tandem mass spectrometry has been recommended, but this is not widely available. Many centers have now adopted the use of gestation-specific reference ranges that are assay and population dependent. In the absence of assay-specific ranges, the Endocrine Society and the American Thyroid Association have recommended TSH reference ranges of 0.1 to 2.5 mIU/L, 0.2 to 3.0 mIU/L, and 0.3 to 3.0 mIU/L in the first, second, and third trimesters, respectively.

During pregnancy, any subnormal TSH value should be evaluated in conjunction with serum free  $ T_{4} $. The diagnosis of clinical hyperthyroidism is confirmed in the presence of suppressed or undetectable serum TSH and elevated serum free  $ T_{4} $. Untreated hyperthyroidism can have severe consequences for the pregnant woman, pregnancy outcome, and the fetus. In the presence of an undetectable or very low serum TSH and elevated serum free  $ T_{4} $, the differential diagnosis in most cases is between Graves hyperthyroidism and gestational transient thyrotoxicosis. The central element in Graves disease is autoimmunity to the TSH receptor, and the hyperthyroidism is caused by TSH receptor-stimulating antibodies. In gestational transient thyrotoxicosis, the thyrotoxicosis is due to high concentrations of hCG stimulating the TSH receptor because of structural homology between hCG and TSH molecules. In Graves disease, thyroid production of  $ T_{3} $ is high and serum total  $ T_{3} $ or serum free  $ T_{3} $ concentrations are typically more elevated than serum total  $ T_{4} $ or free  $ T_{4} $ concentrations. Gestational transient thyrotoxicosis is usually associated with  $ T_{4} $ thyrotoxicosis that can be diagnosed by documenting serum total  $ T_{4} $ or free  $ T_{4} $ levels above the upper normal limit, but with only slightly raised or normal serum total  $ T_{3} $ or free  $ T_{3} $ concentrations.

heat intolerance. In women with hyperemesis gravidarum, control of vomiting and treatment of dehydration with intravenous fluids compose the typical treatment. The patient in this vignette has hypokalemia and correction of electrolyte abnormalities is important. 113 AN Patients presenting with nodular goiter should have a complete history, physical examination, and serum TSH measurement. In this situation, the best recommendation is to aspirate the largest or dominant nodule(s) and to observe the rest with periodic ultrasonography (Answer A). Nodules that have been reported to have benign cytologic characteristics have a false-negative rate of up to 5%; thus, follow-up is recommended within 6 to 18 months of the initial investigation. Although there is no consensus on the definition of clinically significant growth of a thyroid nodule, guidelines from the American Thyroid Association recommend another biopsy when there has been at least a 50% increase in nodule volume within 6 to 18 months from the initial FNAB. If the nodule size is stable, another FNAB is not required and the interval before the next clinical evaluation may be extended to 3 to 5 years.

The American Thyroid Association guidelines provide nodule threshold sizes for FNAB. For mixed cystic-solid nodules such as the ones found in this patient, the recommendation is to aspirate those nodules larger than 2 cm. However, size is not a specific feature to predict risk of malignancy. Since 2 of the 3 mixed cystic-solid nodules in this patient are smaller than 2 cm and have no suspicious features on ultrasonography, performing FNAB on all 3 nodules (Answer B) is incorrect. Notably, the recent guidelines from the British Thyroid Association do not use nodule size as a criterion for FNAB in thyroid nodules. The American Thyroid Association guidelines are currently under revision.

Serum thyroglobulin can be elevated in most thyroid disease and its measurement is a nonsensitive and nonspecific test for thyroid cancer. Although the serum thyroglobulin concentration is useful to monitor patients for residual or recurrent disease after treatment for thyroid cancer, it is not recommended in the initial evaluation of thyroid nodules (thus, Answer C is incorrect). Thyroglobulin antibodies occur in approximately 25% of patients with thyroid cancer and in 10% of the general population.

When present, they may falsely lower serum thyroglobulin concentrations in immunometric assays. Thyroglobulin antibodies should be quantitatively assessed with every thyroglobulin measurement.

Although it is important to follow-up the aspirated and nonaspirated nodules with ultrasonography, it is advisable to aspirate the largest dominant nodule. Ultrasonographic follow-up without aspiration of the largest nodule (Answer D) is not the most appropriate course of action.

In patients with nodular goiter and suppressed serum TSH concentrations, thyroid scanning with either technetium 99m pertechnetate or  $ {}^{123} $I should be performed to assess the functional status of thyroid nodules and to determine the presence of autonomous thyroid nodules. Thyroid scintigraphy (Answer E) has low diagnostic accuracy in the prediction of malignancy in thyroid nodules and is not recommended routinely in patients with normal serum TSH concentrations.
